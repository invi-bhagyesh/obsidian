# 5. Open Sets and Closed Sets in $\mathbb{R}$

> **The topological skeleton of analysis.** Open and closed sets are the *shape* language of analysis. They capture "which points are genuinely interior" and "which points are genuinely on the boundary," letting us define continuity, compactness, and convergence in intrinsic terms — terms that depend only on which sets are open, not on specific numerical formulas.
>
> This chapter develops the standard topology on $\mathbb{R}$ from the ground up: starting with $\varepsilon$-neighbourhoods, defining interior points, open sets, limit points, closure, closed sets, and the boundary. It proves the fundamental stability theorems (arbitrary unions of opens are open; finite intersections of opens are open), derives the sequence characterisation of closedness, and culminates in the structure theorem describing every open set as a countable disjoint union of open intervals.
>
> The material here is prerequisite for compactness, continuity, and the Heine–Borel theorem.

---

## 5.1 Topology on the Real Line

On $\mathbb{R}$ the whole topological structure is generated by **open intervals** $(a, b)$, which play the role of "nearby neighbourhoods." All subsequent definitions in the chapter are ultimately built from this single primitive: the collection of open intervals and finite intersections and arbitrary unions thereof.

The philosophical picture to keep in mind is this: a set $S$ is "open" when every point of $S$ has some breathing room inside $S$ — a small neighbourhood that still lies in $S$. A set is "closed" when it captures every point that its own points can approach in the limit — every accumulation point already lies inside. Openness and closedness are dual concepts (via complementation) but they are *not* opposites: a set can be both (like $\emptyset$ and $\mathbb{R}$), neither (like $\mathbb{Q}$), or exactly one.

## 5.2 Neighbourhoods and Interior Points

### $\varepsilon$-neighbourhood

> **Definition 5.1 ($\varepsilon$-neighbourhood).** For $x_0 \in \mathbb{R}$ and $\varepsilon > 0$, the **$\varepsilon$-neighbourhood** of $x_0$ is
> $$N_\varepsilon(x_0) = (x_0 - \varepsilon,\ x_0 + \varepsilon) = \{x \in \mathbb{R} : |x - x_0| < \varepsilon\}.$$

We also use the term **deleted neighbourhood**:
$$N_\varepsilon^*(x_0) = N_\varepsilon(x_0) \setminus \{x_0\} = \{x \in \mathbb{R} : 0 < |x - x_0| < \varepsilon\}.$$

**Notation conventions.**
- $N_\varepsilon(x_0)$ is always a *symmetric open interval* of radius $\varepsilon$ centred at $x_0$.
- Some texts use $B(x_0, \varepsilon)$ (open ball) or $U_\varepsilon(x_0)$ for the same object. We will stick with $N_\varepsilon$.
- The deleted neighbourhood is used whenever we want to test a *property of approach* — whether a set has points arbitrarily close to $x_0$ other than $x_0$ itself (the definition of limit point).

### Interior points

> **Definition 5.2 (Interior point).** Let $S \subseteq \mathbb{R}$. A point $x \in S$ is an **interior point** of $S$ if there exists $\varepsilon > 0$ with $N_\varepsilon(x) \subseteq S$.

Intuitively: $x$ is interior if it has a whole neighbourhood sitting inside $S$, so $x$ is "thick inside" $S$ rather than perched on an edge.

> **Definition 5.3 (Interior).** The **interior** of $S$ is
> $$\operatorname{int}(S) = S^\circ = \{x \in S : x \text{ is an interior point of } S\}.$$

**Immediate observations.**
- $\operatorname{int}(S) \subseteq S$ by definition.
- $\operatorname{int}(S)$ is the largest open subset of $S$ (Proposition 5.5 below).
- Interior is *monotone*: $A \subseteq B \Rightarrow \operatorname{int}(A) \subseteq \operatorname{int}(B)$.

### Examples

- **$S = [0, 1]$:** $\operatorname{int}(S) = (0, 1)$. For any $x \in (0, 1)$, set $\varepsilon = \min(x, 1 - x) > 0$; then $N_\varepsilon(x) = (x - \varepsilon, x + \varepsilon) \subseteq [0, 1]$. But $0$ is *not* an interior point: for every $\varepsilon > 0$, $N_\varepsilon(0) = (-\varepsilon, \varepsilon)$ contains $-\varepsilon/2 \notin S$. Same for $1$.
- **$S = \mathbb{Q}$:** $\operatorname{int}(\mathbb{Q}) = \emptyset$. Every rational has irrationals arbitrarily close (density of irrationals), so no rational has an $\varepsilon$-neighbourhood sitting inside $\mathbb{Q}$.
- **$S = (0, 1) \cup \{2\}$:** $\operatorname{int}(S) = (0, 1)$. The point $2$ is isolated: every neighbourhood $N_\varepsilon(2) = (2-\varepsilon, 2+\varepsilon)$ contains points like $2 - \varepsilon/2 \notin S$ when $\varepsilon$ is small enough.
- **$S = \mathbb{Z}$:** $\operatorname{int}(\mathbb{Z}) = \emptyset$. Between any two consecutive integers there are non-integer reals, so no integer has an integer-only neighbourhood.

## 5.3 Open Sets

### Definition and basic properties

> **Definition 5.4 (Open set).** $S \subseteq \mathbb{R}$ is **open** if every point of $S$ is an interior point. Equivalently, $S = \operatorname{int}(S)$.

**Convention.** The empty set $\emptyset$ is open vacuously (the universal condition "every point of $\emptyset$ is an interior point" holds because there are no points to check).

> **Proposition 5.5.** $\operatorname{int}(S)$ is the largest open subset of $S$. That is:
> 1. $\operatorname{int}(S)$ is open.
> 2. If $U \subseteq S$ is open, then $U \subseteq \operatorname{int}(S)$.

**Proof.**

**(1)** Take $x \in \operatorname{int}(S)$. By definition there is $\varepsilon > 0$ with $N_\varepsilon(x) \subseteq S$. We claim $N_\varepsilon(x) \subseteq \operatorname{int}(S)$, which shows $x$ is an interior point of $\operatorname{int}(S)$ and hence $\operatorname{int}(S)$ is open.

Let $y \in N_\varepsilon(x)$. Set $\delta = \varepsilon - |y - x| > 0$ (positive since $|y - x| < \varepsilon$). For any $z \in N_\delta(y)$,
$$|z - x| \leq |z - y| + |y - x| < \delta + |y - x| = \varepsilon,$$
so $z \in N_\varepsilon(x) \subseteq S$. Hence $N_\delta(y) \subseteq S$, so $y \in \operatorname{int}(S)$.

**(2)** Take $x \in U$ with $U \subseteq S$ open. Openness of $U$ gives $\varepsilon > 0$ with $N_\varepsilon(x) \subseteq U \subseteq S$. So $x$ is an interior point of $S$, i.e., $x \in \operatorname{int}(S)$. $\blacksquare$

### Examples of open sets

- **Every open interval $(a, b)$ is open.** *Proof.* For $x \in (a, b)$, take $\varepsilon = \min(x - a, b - x) > 0$. Then $N_\varepsilon(x) = (x - \varepsilon, x + \varepsilon)$. For any $y \in N_\varepsilon(x)$: $y > x - \varepsilon \geq x - (x - a) = a$ and $y < x + \varepsilon \leq x + (b - x) = b$, so $y \in (a, b)$. Hence $N_\varepsilon(x) \subseteq (a, b)$. $\blacksquare$
- **$\mathbb{R}$ is open:** every point trivially has every $\varepsilon$-neighbourhood inside $\mathbb{R}$.
- **$(-\infty, a)$ is open:** for $x < a$, take $\varepsilon = a - x > 0$, and $N_\varepsilon(x) = (x - \varepsilon, x + \varepsilon)$. The right endpoint $x + \varepsilon = a$ is excluded, so $N_\varepsilon(x) \subseteq (-\infty, a)$.
- **$(a, \infty)$ is open:** symmetric argument.
- **$\emptyset$ is open** (vacuous).
- **$[a, b]$ is NOT open:** $a$ is not an interior point, since every $N_\varepsilon(a)$ contains $a - \varepsilon/2 < a$, which is outside $[a, b]$.
- **$\mathbb{Q}$ is NOT open:** no rational is interior to $\mathbb{Q}$ because every neighbourhood of a rational contains irrationals.
- **$[a, b)$ is NOT open:** $a$ is not interior (same reason as $[a,b]$).

### Fundamental theorem on open sets

> **Theorem 5.6 (Axioms of the standard topology on $\mathbb{R}$).** The class $\tau$ of open subsets of $\mathbb{R}$ satisfies:
> 1. $\emptyset \in \tau$ and $\mathbb{R} \in \tau$.
> 2. **Arbitrary unions of opens are open:** if $\{U_\alpha\}_{\alpha \in I}$ is any family of open sets (indexed by an arbitrary — possibly uncountable — set $I$), then $\bigcup_{\alpha \in I} U_\alpha$ is open.
> 3. **Finite intersections of opens are open:** if $U_1, \ldots, U_n$ are open, then $\bigcap_{i=1}^n U_i$ is open.

**Proof.**

**(1)** Already verified above.

**(2)** *Setup.* Let $U = \bigcup_{\alpha \in I} U_\alpha$. We show every $x \in U$ is an interior point.

*Argument.* Fix $x \in U$. By definition of union, $x \in U_{\alpha_0}$ for some index $\alpha_0 \in I$. Since $U_{\alpha_0}$ is open, there exists $\varepsilon > 0$ with
$$N_\varepsilon(x) \subseteq U_{\alpha_0}.$$
But $U_{\alpha_0} \subseteq \bigcup_\alpha U_\alpha = U$, so
$$N_\varepsilon(x) \subseteq U.$$
Thus $x$ is an interior point of $U$. Since $x$ was arbitrary, $U$ is open.

**(3)** *Setup.* Let $U = \bigcap_{i=1}^n U_i$. We show every $x \in U$ is an interior point.

*Argument.* Fix $x \in U$. Then $x \in U_i$ for *every* $i = 1, \ldots, n$. Openness of each $U_i$ gives $\varepsilon_i > 0$ with
$$N_{\varepsilon_i}(x) \subseteq U_i.$$
Set
$$\varepsilon = \min(\varepsilon_1, \varepsilon_2, \ldots, \varepsilon_n).$$
This is well-defined because the min of *finitely many* positive numbers is positive — crucially, $\varepsilon > 0$. (An infinite infimum of positive reals can be zero, as in $\inf\{1/n : n \in \mathbb{N}\} = 0$; this is exactly where the proof would break.)

Now $N_\varepsilon(x) \subseteq N_{\varepsilon_i}(x) \subseteq U_i$ for every $i$, so
$$N_\varepsilon(x) \subseteq \bigcap_{i=1}^n U_i = U.$$
Hence $x$ is interior to $U$, proving $U$ is open. $\blacksquare$

**Warning (why "finitely many" is essential).** Consider
$$\bigcap_{n=1}^\infty \left(-\tfrac{1}{n}, \tfrac{1}{n}\right) = \{0\}.$$
Each $(-1/n, 1/n)$ is open, but their infinite intersection is the singleton $\{0\}$, which is *not* open ($0$ has no open neighbourhood inside $\{0\}$). In the proof above, $\varepsilon_n = 1/n \to 0$, so $\inf_n \varepsilon_n = 0$ and we cannot extract a positive $\varepsilon$.

**Topological generalisation.** The three properties in Theorem 5.6 are taken as *axioms* for a **topology** on an abstract set $X$: a topology $\tau$ on $X$ is a family of subsets containing $\emptyset, X$, closed under arbitrary unions and finite intersections. The case $X = \mathbb{R}$ with $\tau$ = open subsets in the sense above is the **standard topology** on $\mathbb{R}$.

### Structure theorem

> **Theorem 5.7 (Structure of open sets in $\mathbb{R}$).** Every non-empty open subset $U \subseteq \mathbb{R}$ can be written as a **countable** (possibly finite) disjoint union of open intervals:
> $$U = \bigsqcup_{k} I_k, \qquad I_k = (a_k, b_k) \text{ or } (-\infty, b_k) \text{ or } (a_k, \infty) \text{ or } \mathbb{R}.$$
> The decomposition is unique up to reordering of components.

**Proof.**

*Step 1: Define components.* For each $x \in U$, consider all open intervals $J$ with $x \in J \subseteq U$. Such intervals exist (openness gives $N_\varepsilon(x) \subseteq U$). Let
$$I_x = \bigcup\{J : J \text{ open interval},\ x \in J \subseteq U\}.$$

*Step 2: $I_x$ is an open interval.* $I_x$ is a union of open intervals all containing $x$. The union of a family of intervals with common point is an interval (its sup and inf give the endpoints). And arbitrary unions of opens are open (Theorem 5.6(2)), so $I_x$ is an open interval. By construction $x \in I_x \subseteq U$, and $I_x$ is the *largest* open interval with these properties.

*Step 3: Components partition $U$.* Claim: for $x, y \in U$, either $I_x = I_y$ or $I_x \cap I_y = \emptyset$. Suppose $I_x \cap I_y \neq \emptyset$. Then $I_x \cup I_y$ is connected (union of two overlapping intervals is an interval), open, and contained in $U$. So $I_x \cup I_y$ is an open interval containing $x$ and contained in $U$, hence $I_x \cup I_y \subseteq I_x$ by maximality. Symmetrically $\subseteq I_y$. So $I_x = I_y$.

*Step 4: Countability.* Each component $I_k$ is a non-empty open interval, hence contains a rational (density of $\mathbb{Q}$). Distinct components are disjoint, so the rationals they contain are distinct. The map (component) $\mapsto$ (some chosen rational in it) is therefore injective into $\mathbb{Q}$, a countable set. So the number of components is at most $|\mathbb{Q}| = \aleph_0$.

*Step 5: Uniqueness.* If $U = \bigsqcup_k I_k$ with each $I_k$ a maximal open interval in $U$, then $I_k = I_x$ for any $x \in I_k$ — the components are determined by $U$. $\blacksquare$

**Remark.** This is a specifically one-dimensional phenomenon. In $\mathbb{R}^n$ for $n \geq 2$, open sets are countable disjoint unions of *connected components*, but those components need not be as simple as intervals (they can be arbitrary open connected sets).

## 5.4 Limit Points and Adherent Points

### Limit points

> **Definition 5.8 (Limit point).** Let $S \subseteq \mathbb{R}$. A point $x \in \mathbb{R}$ (not necessarily in $S$) is a **limit point** (or **accumulation point** or **cluster point**) of $S$ if every $\varepsilon$-neighbourhood of $x$ contains a point of $S$ other than $x$:
> $$\forall \varepsilon > 0,\ \exists s \in S \text{ with } 0 < |s - x| < \varepsilon.$$
> The set of all limit points of $S$ is denoted $S'$ and called the **derived set**.

Equivalently: every deleted neighbourhood $N_\varepsilon^*(x)$ meets $S$.

**Reformulation.** $x$ is a limit point of $S$ iff there is a sequence $(x_n) \subseteq S$ with $x_n \neq x$ and $x_n \to x$. (This becomes Theorem 5.14 below once we formally have the sequence characterisation.)

### Adherent points (closure points)

> **Definition 5.9 (Adherent point).** $x \in \mathbb{R}$ is an **adherent point** (or **closure point**) of $S$ if every $\varepsilon$-neighbourhood of $x$ contains a point of $S$:
> $$\forall \varepsilon > 0,\ \exists s \in S \text{ with } |s - x| < \varepsilon.$$

**Key relationships.**
- Every element of $S$ is adherent to $S$: take $s = x \in S$.
- Every limit point of $S$ is adherent: a point $s \neq x$ with $|s - x| < \varepsilon$ is a fortiori a point with $|s - x| < \varepsilon$.
- The converse fails: an isolated point of $S$ (defined below) is adherent but not a limit point.

### Isolated points

> **Definition 5.10 (Isolated point).** $x \in S$ is an **isolated point** of $S$ if there exists $\varepsilon > 0$ with
> $$N_\varepsilon(x) \cap S = \{x\}.$$

**Classification.** Every point of $S$ is either a limit point of $S$ or an isolated point of $S$, and never both:
- If $x \in S$ is *not* a limit point, then some $\varepsilon$ has $N_\varepsilon^*(x) \cap S = \emptyset$, so $N_\varepsilon(x) \cap S = \{x\}$, making $x$ isolated.
- If $x$ is isolated, the $\varepsilon$ witnessing isolation shows $x$ is not a limit point.

### Examples

- **$S = (0, 1)$:** 
  - Limit points $S' = [0, 1]$. Every $x \in (0, 1)$ is a limit point (nearby rationals in $(0,1)$); $0, 1$ are limit points (approachable from inside $(0,1)$) though they are not elements of $S$.
  - Adherent points: $\overline{S} = [0, 1]$.
  - Isolated points: none (no point $x \in (0,1)$ has a neighbourhood meeting $(0,1)$ only at $x$).
- **$S = \{1/n : n \in \mathbb{N}\}$:** 
  - Limit points $S' = \{0\}$. Indeed, $1/n \to 0$ gives $0 \in S'$. And each $1/n$ is isolated: pick $\varepsilon = \tfrac{1}{2}\left(\tfrac{1}{n} - \tfrac{1}{n+1}\right) > 0$, then $N_\varepsilon(1/n) \cap S = \{1/n\}$.
  - Adherent points: $S \cup \{0\}$.
  - Isolated points: every element of $S$.
- **$S = \mathbb{Z}$:**
  - Limit points $S' = \emptyset$. For any $n \in \mathbb{Z}$, $N_{1/2}(n) \cap \mathbb{Z} = \{n\}$, so no integer is a limit point; and for any $x \notin \mathbb{Z}$, the distance to the nearest integer is positive, so some neighbourhood avoids $\mathbb{Z}$.
  - Adherent points: $\mathbb{Z}$.
  - Isolated points: every integer.
- **$S = \mathbb{Q}$:**
  - Limit points $S' = \mathbb{R}$ (density of $\mathbb{Q}$).
  - Adherent points: $\mathbb{R}$.
  - Isolated points: none.

## 5.5 Closure of a Set

> **Definition 5.11 (Closure).** The **closure** of $S$ is the set of all adherent points:
> $$\overline{S} = \{x \in \mathbb{R} : \forall \varepsilon > 0,\ N_\varepsilon(x) \cap S \neq \emptyset\}.$$

Equivalently, $\overline{S} = S \cup S'$: every adherent point is either in $S$ itself (hence witnesses its own $\varepsilon$-condition by $s = x$) or is a limit point of $S$.

### Properties

> **Proposition 5.12 (Kuratowski-style properties of closure).** For $A, B \subseteq \mathbb{R}$:
> 1. $S \subseteq \overline{S}$.
> 2. $\overline{\emptyset} = \emptyset$.
> 3. $\overline{\overline{S}} = \overline{S}$ (idempotence).
> 4. **Monotonicity:** $A \subseteq B \Rightarrow \overline{A} \subseteq \overline{B}$.
> 5. **Finite additivity:** $\overline{A \cup B} = \overline{A} \cup \overline{B}$.
> 6. $\overline{A \cap B} \subseteq \overline{A} \cap \overline{B}$, and **strict inclusion is possible**.

**Proof.**

**(1)** Every $x \in S$ is adherent (take $s = x$), so $S \subseteq \overline{S}$.

**(2)** No point can be adherent to the empty set: the condition "$\exists s \in \emptyset$" is vacuously false.

**(3)** We show $\overline{\overline{S}} \subseteq \overline{S}$ (the reverse inclusion is (1) applied to $\overline{S}$).

Take $x \in \overline{\overline{S}}$. For any $\varepsilon > 0$, there exists $y \in \overline{S}$ with $|y - x| < \varepsilon/2$. Since $y \in \overline{S}$, there exists $s \in S$ with $|s - y| < \varepsilon/2$. By the triangle inequality,
$$|s - x| \leq |s - y| + |y - x| < \varepsilon/2 + \varepsilon/2 = \varepsilon.$$
So $N_\varepsilon(x) \cap S \neq \emptyset$, i.e., $x \in \overline{S}$.

**(4)** Take $x \in \overline{A}$. For every $\varepsilon > 0$, $N_\varepsilon(x) \cap A \neq \emptyset$. Since $A \subseteq B$, $N_\varepsilon(x) \cap B \supseteq N_\varepsilon(x) \cap A \neq \emptyset$. So $x \in \overline{B}$.

**(5)** *($\supseteq$)* $A \subseteq A \cup B$ so by (4) $\overline{A} \subseteq \overline{A \cup B}$; similarly $\overline{B} \subseteq \overline{A \cup B}$. Taking unions: $\overline{A} \cup \overline{B} \subseteq \overline{A \cup B}$.

*($\subseteq$)* Contrapositive: suppose $x \notin \overline{A} \cup \overline{B}$. Then $x \notin \overline{A}$ and $x \notin \overline{B}$, so there are $\varepsilon_1, \varepsilon_2 > 0$ with
$$N_{\varepsilon_1}(x) \cap A = \emptyset, \qquad N_{\varepsilon_2}(x) \cap B = \emptyset.$$
Let $\varepsilon = \min(\varepsilon_1, \varepsilon_2) > 0$. Then $N_\varepsilon(x) \cap A = \emptyset$ and $N_\varepsilon(x) \cap B = \emptyset$, so $N_\varepsilon(x) \cap (A \cup B) = \emptyset$, i.e., $x \notin \overline{A \cup B}$.

**(6)** $A \cap B \subseteq A$, so $\overline{A \cap B} \subseteq \overline{A}$; similarly $\overline{A \cap B} \subseteq \overline{B}$. Hence $\overline{A \cap B} \subseteq \overline{A} \cap \overline{B}$.

*Example of strict inclusion.* Let $A = \mathbb{Q}$, $B = \mathbb{R} \setminus \mathbb{Q}$. Then $A \cap B = \emptyset$, so $\overline{A \cap B} = \emptyset$; but $\overline{A} = \overline{B} = \mathbb{R}$, whose intersection is $\mathbb{R}$. $\blacksquare$

## 5.6 Closed Sets

### Definition

> **Definition 5.13 (Closed set).** $S \subseteq \mathbb{R}$ is **closed** if it contains all its limit points — equivalently, $S = \overline{S}$ — equivalently, $S' \subseteq S$.

**Unpacking the equivalence.** $S = \overline{S} = S \cup S'$ iff $S' \subseteq S$, since $S \subseteq S$ is automatic.

### Relationship with open sets (topological duality)

> **Theorem 5.14 (Open/closed duality).** $S \subseteq \mathbb{R}$ is closed $\iff$ its complement $S^c = \mathbb{R} \setminus S$ is open.

**Proof.**

**($\Rightarrow$)** Suppose $S$ is closed. We show $S^c$ is open: every point of $S^c$ is interior to $S^c$.

Take $x \in S^c$, i.e., $x \notin S$. If $x$ were a limit point of $S$, then by definition of closed $x \in S$, a contradiction. So $x$ is not a limit point of $S$.

This means: there exists $\varepsilon > 0$ such that the deleted neighbourhood $N_\varepsilon^*(x)$ does not meet $S$, i.e., $N_\varepsilon^*(x) \cap S = \emptyset$. But $x \notin S$ also (our starting assumption), so even the undeleted neighbourhood satisfies $N_\varepsilon(x) \cap S = \emptyset$, i.e.,
$$N_\varepsilon(x) \subseteq S^c.$$
Hence $x$ is interior to $S^c$. Since $x$ was arbitrary, $S^c$ is open.

**($\Leftarrow$)** Suppose $S^c$ is open. Let $x$ be a limit point of $S$; we must show $x \in S$.

Suppose for contradiction $x \notin S$, i.e., $x \in S^c$. By openness of $S^c$, there exists $\varepsilon > 0$ with $N_\varepsilon(x) \subseteq S^c$, equivalently $N_\varepsilon(x) \cap S = \emptyset$. But then the deleted neighbourhood $N_\varepsilon^*(x) \cap S \subseteq N_\varepsilon(x) \cap S = \emptyset$, contradicting that $x$ is a limit point of $S$.

So $x \in S$, and $S$ contains all its limit points, i.e., $S$ is closed. $\blacksquare$

### Examples

- **$[a, b]$ is closed.** Complement $\mathbb{R} \setminus [a, b] = (-\infty, a) \cup (b, \infty)$ is a union of two open intervals, hence open (Theorem 5.6(2)). So $[a, b]$ is closed.
- **$[a, \infty)$, $(-\infty, b]$ are closed** (complements are open half-lines).
- **Singletons $\{x_0\}$ are closed.** Complement $(-\infty, x_0) \cup (x_0, \infty)$ is open.
- **$\mathbb{Z}$ is closed.** We saw $\mathbb{Z}' = \emptyset$, so $\mathbb{Z} \supseteq \mathbb{Z}'$ vacuously. Alternatively, $\mathbb{R} \setminus \mathbb{Z} = \bigcup_{n \in \mathbb{Z}} (n, n+1)$ is a countable union of opens.
- **$\emptyset$ and $\mathbb{R}$ are both open AND closed.** $\emptyset^c = \mathbb{R}$ is open, so $\emptyset$ is closed; and $\emptyset$ is open by convention. Similarly for $\mathbb{R}$. These are the only two **clopen** subsets of $\mathbb{R}$ (a consequence of connectedness).
- **$\mathbb{Q}$ is neither open nor closed.** Not open: $\operatorname{int}(\mathbb{Q}) = \emptyset \neq \mathbb{Q}$. Not closed: $\overline{\mathbb{Q}} = \mathbb{R} \neq \mathbb{Q}$ (every irrational is a limit point of $\mathbb{Q}$ but not in $\mathbb{Q}$).
- **$\{1/n : n \in \mathbb{N}\}$ is neither open nor closed.** Not open: no point is interior. Not closed: $0$ is a limit point but $0 \notin S$.
- **$\{0\} \cup \{1/n : n \in \mathbb{N}\}$ is closed.** Adding the missing limit point repairs it.

### Dual theorem

> **Theorem 5.15 (Axioms of closed sets).** The class $\mathcal{F}$ of closed subsets of $\mathbb{R}$ satisfies:
> 1. $\emptyset \in \mathcal{F}$ and $\mathbb{R} \in \mathcal{F}$.
> 2. **Arbitrary intersections of closeds are closed:** if $\{C_\alpha\}_{\alpha \in I}$ are closed, $\bigcap_\alpha C_\alpha$ is closed.
> 3. **Finite unions of closeds are closed:** if $C_1, \ldots, C_n$ are closed, $\bigcup_{i=1}^n C_i$ is closed.

**Proof.** Apply De Morgan's laws to Theorem 5.6.

**(1)** $\emptyset^c = \mathbb{R}$ and $\mathbb{R}^c = \emptyset$, both open.

**(2)**
$$\mathbb{R} \setminus \bigcap_\alpha C_\alpha = \bigcup_\alpha (\mathbb{R} \setminus C_\alpha).$$
Each $\mathbb{R} \setminus C_\alpha$ is open, so the union is open (Theorem 5.6(2)), hence the original intersection is closed.

**(3)**
$$\mathbb{R} \setminus \bigcup_{i=1}^n C_i = \bigcap_{i=1}^n (\mathbb{R} \setminus C_i).$$
Each $\mathbb{R} \setminus C_i$ is open, and finite intersections of opens are open (Theorem 5.6(3)), so the complement of the union is open, i.e., the union is closed. $\blacksquare$

**Warning (why "finitely many" is essential).** $\bigcup_{n \in \mathbb{N}} [1/n, 1] = (0, 1]$ is *not* closed — each $[1/n, 1]$ is closed, but the infinite union loses $0$ as a limit point, or equivalently fails to contain its limit $0$ (note the union is $(0, 1]$, and $0$ is a limit point not in the set). This mirrors the warning for open sets.

## 5.7 Characterisations via Sequences

Once we have sequences in hand (see [[08-sequences-introduction]]), closures and closedness admit purely sequential characterisations, which are often the most efficient way to verify them in proofs.

> **Theorem 5.16 (Sequential characterisation of closure).** Let $S \subseteq \mathbb{R}$ and $x \in \mathbb{R}$. Then $x \in \overline{S}$ if and only if there exists a sequence $(x_n)$ with $x_n \in S$ for all $n$ and $x_n \to x$.

**Proof.**

**($\Rightarrow$)** Suppose $x \in \overline{S}$. For each $n \in \mathbb{N}$, apply the adherence condition with $\varepsilon = 1/n$: there exists $x_n \in S$ with $|x_n - x| < 1/n$.

We claim $x_n \to x$. Given $\varepsilon > 0$, choose $N$ with $1/N < \varepsilon$ (Archimedean property). Then for all $n \geq N$,
$$|x_n - x| < \tfrac{1}{n} \leq \tfrac{1}{N} < \varepsilon.$$
So $x_n \to x$.

**($\Leftarrow$)** Suppose $(x_n) \subseteq S$ and $x_n \to x$. We show $x \in \overline{S}$: for every $\varepsilon > 0$, some element of $S$ lies in $N_\varepsilon(x)$.

Given $\varepsilon > 0$, convergence gives $N$ such that $|x_n - x| < \varepsilon$ for $n \geq N$. Pick $n = N$: $x_N \in S$ and $|x_N - x| < \varepsilon$, i.e., $x_N \in N_\varepsilon(x) \cap S$. So the intersection is non-empty. $\blacksquare$

> **Theorem 5.17 (Sequential characterisation of closedness).** $S \subseteq \mathbb{R}$ is closed if and only if: for every convergent sequence $(x_n)$ with $x_n \in S$ for all $n$, the limit $\lim x_n$ lies in $S$.

**Proof.**

**($\Rightarrow$)** Assume $S$ is closed. Suppose $(x_n) \subseteq S$ with $x_n \to x$. By Theorem 5.16, $x \in \overline{S}$. Since $S$ is closed, $\overline{S} = S$, so $x \in S$.

**($\Leftarrow$)** Assume the sequential condition. We show $\overline{S} \subseteq S$ (which combined with $S \subseteq \overline{S}$ gives $S = \overline{S}$, i.e., $S$ closed).

Take $x \in \overline{S}$. By Theorem 5.16, there is a sequence $(x_n) \subseteq S$ with $x_n \to x$. By the hypothesis, $x \in S$. Hence $\overline{S} \subseteq S$. $\blacksquare$

**Interpretation.** "Closed = sequentially closed" on $\mathbb{R}$. This is a special feature of metric spaces (and more generally first-countable spaces): in general topological spaces, sequential closure can be strictly smaller than topological closure, and one must use *nets* or *filters* to get the general characterisation.

## 5.8 Boundary of a Set

> **Definition 5.18 (Boundary).** The **boundary** of $S \subseteq \mathbb{R}$ is
> $$\partial S = \overline{S} \cap \overline{S^c} = \overline{S} \cap \overline{\mathbb{R} \setminus S}.$$

**Equivalent characterisations.**
- $x \in \partial S \iff$ every neighbourhood of $x$ meets both $S$ and $S^c$:
$$\forall \varepsilon > 0,\ N_\varepsilon(x) \cap S \neq \emptyset \text{ and } N_\varepsilon(x) \cap S^c \neq \emptyset.$$
- $\partial S = \overline{S} \setminus \operatorname{int}(S)$.
- $\partial S = \partial(S^c)$.

**Proof of $\partial S = \overline{S} \setminus \operatorname{int}(S)$.** 

*($\supseteq$)* Take $x \in \overline{S} \setminus \operatorname{int}(S)$. Then $x \in \overline{S}$, so $N_\varepsilon(x) \cap S \neq \emptyset$ for all $\varepsilon$. Also $x \notin \operatorname{int}(S)$, so $x$ is not an interior point of $S$; hence no $N_\varepsilon(x)$ is contained in $S$, meaning every $N_\varepsilon(x) \cap S^c \neq \emptyset$. So $x \in \overline{S^c}$. Combined: $x \in \partial S$.

*($\subseteq$)* Take $x \in \partial S = \overline{S} \cap \overline{S^c}$. Then $x \in \overline{S}$, so we need only show $x \notin \operatorname{int}(S)$. If $x \in \operatorname{int}(S)$, some $N_\varepsilon(x) \subseteq S$, which contradicts $x \in \overline{S^c}$. So $x \notin \operatorname{int}(S)$. $\blacksquare$

**Disjoint decomposition.** $\overline{S} = \operatorname{int}(S) \sqcup \partial S$, and dually $\mathbb{R} = \operatorname{int}(S) \sqcup \partial S \sqcup \operatorname{int}(S^c)$.

### Examples

- **$\partial [0, 1] = \partial (0, 1) = \partial [0, 1) = \{0, 1\}$.** The boundary depends only on $\overline{S}$ and $\operatorname{int}(S)$, and all four intervals have the same closure $[0,1]$ and same interior $(0,1)$.
- **$\partial \mathbb{Q} = \mathbb{R}$.** $\overline{\mathbb{Q}} = \mathbb{R}$, $\operatorname{int}(\mathbb{Q}) = \emptyset$, so $\partial \mathbb{Q} = \mathbb{R}$. Every real is on the boundary of $\mathbb{Q}$.
- **$\partial \mathbb{Z} = \mathbb{Z}$.** $\overline{\mathbb{Z}} = \mathbb{Z}$, $\operatorname{int}(\mathbb{Z}) = \emptyset$, so $\partial \mathbb{Z} = \mathbb{Z}$.
- **$\partial \emptyset = \partial \mathbb{R} = \emptyset$.** The clopen sets have empty boundary.

## 5.9 Dense Sets

> **Definition 5.19 (Dense set).** $D \subseteq \mathbb{R}$ is **dense in $\mathbb{R}$** if $\overline{D} = \mathbb{R}$.

**Equivalent characterisations.**
- Every non-empty open interval $(a, b)$ contains a point of $D$.
- Every non-empty open set contains a point of $D$.
- For every $x \in \mathbb{R}$ and every $\varepsilon > 0$, $N_\varepsilon(x) \cap D \neq \emptyset$.
- For every $x \in \mathbb{R}$, there is a sequence $(d_n) \subseteq D$ with $d_n \to x$.

**Classical examples.**
- **$\mathbb{Q}$ is dense in $\mathbb{R}$** (density theorem, Chapter 3 [[03-supremum-and-infimum]]).
- **$\mathbb{R} \setminus \mathbb{Q}$ is dense in $\mathbb{R}$**: given $(a, b)$, pick rational $q \in (a, b)$ (density of $\mathbb{Q}$) and irrational $\sqrt{2}/n$ for small $n$ — shifted $q + \sqrt{2}/n$ is irrational and still in $(a, b)$ for $n$ large enough.
- **Dyadic rationals $\{m/2^n : m \in \mathbb{Z}, n \in \mathbb{N}_0\}$ are dense.** Given $(a, b)$, pick $n$ with $2^n > 1/(b - a)$; then consecutive dyadics $m/2^n, (m+1)/2^n$ are within $1/2^n < b - a$, forcing at least one to land in $(a, b)$.
- **$\{n\alpha \bmod 1 : n \in \mathbb{N}\}$ is dense in $[0, 1]$** whenever $\alpha$ is irrational (Weyl equidistribution).

**Relevance.** Density is the structural reason that $\mathbb{Q}$-valued information (which is countable) determines $\mathbb{R}$-valued continuous functions — a central theme in approximation theory (Weierstrass, Stone-Weierstrass) and in the construction of $\mathbb{R}$ from $\mathbb{Q}$ via Cauchy sequences or Dedekind cuts.

## Worked Examples

### Example 1: Full topological analysis of a set with isolated points

Determine whether $S = \{1/n : n \in \mathbb{N}\} \cup \{0\}$ is open, closed, or neither. Find $\operatorname{int}(S)$, $S'$, $\overline{S}$, $\partial S$.

**Setup.** $S = \{0, 1, 1/2, 1/3, 1/4, \ldots\}$, a countably infinite set clustering at $0$.

**Strategy.** Test each topological invariant in turn: interior (do points have open neighbourhoods in $S$?), derived set (what are the limit points?), closure (add limit points), boundary (closure minus interior).

**Computation.**

*Interior:* $\operatorname{int}(S) = \emptyset$. Consider any point $p \in S$.
- If $p = 1/n$: every neighbourhood $N_\varepsilon(1/n)$ contains irrational numbers (density of irrationals); irrationals are not in $S$, so $N_\varepsilon(1/n) \not\subseteq S$.
- If $p = 0$: every $N_\varepsilon(0) = (-\varepsilon, \varepsilon)$ contains $-\varepsilon/2 \notin S$.

So no point is interior.

*Derived set:* $S' = \{0\}$.
- $0$ is a limit point: $1/n \to 0$ with $1/n \neq 0 \in S$, so every $N_\varepsilon(0)$ contains some $1/n \neq 0$.
- Each $1/n$ is isolated: pick $\varepsilon_n = \tfrac{1}{2}\min(\tfrac{1}{n} - \tfrac{1}{n+1},\ \tfrac{1}{n-1} - \tfrac{1}{n}) > 0$ (for $n \geq 2$; for $n = 1$ use $\varepsilon_1 = 1/4$). Then $N_{\varepsilon_n}(1/n) \cap S = \{1/n\}$, so $1/n$ is not a limit point.
- No point outside $S \cup \{0\}$ is a limit point: if $x \notin S$ and $x \neq 0$, then the distance from $x$ to $S$ is positive (since $S$ is a discrete set clustering only at $0$), so some neighbourhood avoids $S$.

*Closure:* $\overline{S} = S \cup S' = S$ (since $0 \in S$ already).

*Boundary:* $\partial S = \overline{S} \setminus \operatorname{int}(S) = S \setminus \emptyset = S$.

**Verification.** Since $\overline{S} = S$, $S$ is closed (Definition 5.13). Complement $S^c = (-\infty, 0) \cup (\bigcup_n (1/(n+1), 1/n)) \cup (1, \infty)$ is a union of open intervals, hence open — confirming closedness via Theorem 5.14. Not open because $\operatorname{int}(S) = \emptyset \neq S$.

**Interpretation.** $S$ illustrates a *convergent sequence together with its limit*. Adding the limit point $0$ is exactly what makes this closed — remove it and we get the classic non-closed example $\{1/n\}$. Every point is on the boundary because every point has both $S$-neighbours (within $S$) and non-$S$ neighbours (irrationals). The set is discrete (no interior) but topologically tight (closed).

**Status.** Closed, not open. $\operatorname{int}(S) = \emptyset$, $S' = \{0\}$, $\overline{S} = S$, $\partial S = S$. $\blacksquare$

### Example 2: Union of two open intervals

Let $A = (0, 1) \cup (2, 3)$. Find $\operatorname{int}(A)$, $A'$, $\overline{A}$, $\partial A$. Is $A$ open? Closed?

**Setup.** Two disjoint open intervals separated by a gap.

**Strategy.** Use the arbitrary-union-of-opens theorem to dispatch openness immediately, then reason about limit points interval-by-interval.

**Computation.**

*Openness:* $A$ is a union of two open sets $(0, 1)$ and $(2, 3)$, hence open (Theorem 5.6(2)). So $\operatorname{int}(A) = A$.

*Derived set $A'$:* Every limit point of $A$ is a limit point of $(0, 1)$ or $(2, 3)$, and conversely. For $(a, b)$, the derived set is $[a, b]$ (every $x \in [a,b]$ is approached by a sequence in $(a,b)$).

So $A' = [0, 1] \cup [2, 3]$.

*Closure:* $\overline{A} = A \cup A' = [0, 1] \cup [2, 3]$.

*Boundary:* $\partial A = \overline{A} \setminus \operatorname{int}(A) = ([0,1] \cup [2,3]) \setminus ((0,1) \cup (2,3)) = \{0, 1, 2, 3\}$.

**Verification.** $\overline{A} \neq A$ (the endpoints $0, 1, 2, 3$ are in $\overline{A}$ but not in $A$), so $A$ is not closed. $A$ is open (established above). This is consistent with the general fact that most sets are neither open nor closed; but being *open and not closed* is the norm for most open sets of interest (except $\emptyset, \mathbb{R}$).

**Interpretation.** An open set's closure adjoins all the "limit endpoints." The boundary exposes the topological edges where interior meets exterior.

**Status.** Open, not closed. $\blacksquare$

### Example 3: The Cantor set is closed

Prove that the Cantor middle-thirds set $C \subseteq [0, 1]$ is closed.

**Setup.** Construct $C$ as the intersection
$$C = \bigcap_{n = 0}^\infty C_n,$$
where $C_0 = [0, 1]$ and $C_{n+1}$ is obtained from $C_n$ by removing the open middle third of each component interval. So
- $C_1 = [0, 1/3] \cup [2/3, 1]$,
- $C_2 = [0, 1/9] \cup [2/9, 1/3] \cup [2/3, 7/9] \cup [8/9, 1]$,
- $\ldots$

**Strategy.** Each $C_n$ is a finite union of closed intervals, hence closed. An arbitrary intersection of closed sets is closed.

**Computation.**

*Step 1.* $C_n$ is closed for every $n \geq 0$. Each $C_n$ is a finite disjoint union of closed intervals $[a_k^{(n)}, b_k^{(n)}]$. Each interval $[a, b]$ is closed (complement $(-\infty, a) \cup (b, \infty)$ is open). Finite unions of closed sets are closed (Theorem 5.15(3)). So $C_n$ is closed.

*Step 2.* $C = \bigcap_{n=0}^\infty C_n$ is an arbitrary (countably infinite) intersection of closed sets.

*Step 3.* Arbitrary intersections of closed sets are closed (Theorem 5.15(2)). Hence $C$ is closed.

**Verification.** Alternatively, $C = [0, 1] \setminus \bigcup_{n=1}^\infty G_n$ where $G_n$ is the union of open middle-thirds removed at stage $n$. Each $G_n$ is open (finite union of open intervals) so $\bigcup G_n$ is open (Theorem 5.6(2)). Hence $[0, 1] \setminus \bigcup G_n$ is the intersection of the closed set $[0,1]$ with the closed set $\mathbb{R} \setminus \bigcup G_n$, hence closed.

**Interpretation.** The Cantor set has remarkable properties that all follow from closedness combined with other features: it is compact (closed and bounded), uncountable (cardinality $2^{\aleph_0}$), totally disconnected (no non-trivial intervals), has empty interior and Lebesgue measure zero, yet is perfect (every point is a limit point of the set itself). It is the canonical "fractal" and serves as a source of examples and counterexamples throughout analysis. $\blacksquare$

### Example 4: Sine positivity set is open but not closed

Show that $S = \{x \in \mathbb{R} : \sin x > 0\}$ is open but not closed.

**Setup.** $\sin x > 0$ exactly when $x$ lies in one of the "positive humps" of the sine curve: $x \in (2k\pi, (2k+1)\pi)$ for some $k \in \mathbb{Z}$.

**Strategy.** Write $S$ explicitly as a union of open intervals (open), then find a limit point not in $S$ (not closed).

**Computation.**

*Step 1 (openness).* Since $\sin x > 0$ iff $x \in (2k\pi, (2k+1)\pi)$ for some $k \in \mathbb{Z}$,
$$S = \bigcup_{k \in \mathbb{Z}} (2k\pi,\ (2k+1)\pi).$$
This is a countable union of open intervals. By Theorem 5.6(2), $S$ is open.

*Alternative proof via continuity.* The function $\sin: \mathbb{R} \to \mathbb{R}$ is continuous; $S = \sin^{-1}((0, \infty))$ is the preimage of an open set under a continuous function. By the topological characterisation of continuity (see [[16-continuity]]), the preimage of an open set under a continuous function is open.

*Step 2 (not closed).* Consider the point $x_0 = \pi$. We have $\sin(\pi) = 0 \not> 0$, so $\pi \notin S$. But $\pi$ is a limit point of $S$: take $x_n = \pi - 1/n$. Then $x_n \in (0, \pi) \subseteq S$ for $n$ large, and $x_n \to \pi$. By Theorem 5.16 (or the definition), $\pi \in S'$. Since $\pi \in S' \setminus S$, $S$ is not closed.

**Verification.** The complement $S^c = \{x : \sin x \leq 0\} = \bigcup_{k \in \mathbb{Z}} [(2k - 1)\pi,\ 2k\pi]$ is a union of closed intervals, but *not* closed either! Well, actually the complement *is* closed: it is the preimage of the closed set $(-\infty, 0]$ under the continuous sine function. (The union of those closed intervals is closed because they line up to tile $\mathbb{R}$ with a shift, and more rigorously because the set is defined by the non-strict inequality.)

Actually let's double-check: $S^c$ closed iff $S$ open — and we showed $S$ is open, so $S^c$ is closed. Consistent.

**Interpretation.** This is a prototypical example of an open set defined by a strict inequality applied to a continuous function. The same principle generalises: $\{x : f(x) > c\}$ is open whenever $f$ is continuous, for any constant $c$. The boundary $\partial S = \pi\mathbb{Z}$ is where $\sin$ hits zero — the transition locus. $\blacksquare$

### Example 5: Every finite subset is closed

Prove that every finite subset $F = \{a_1, a_2, \ldots, a_n\}$ of $\mathbb{R}$ is closed.

**Setup.** A finite collection of real numbers.

**Strategy.** Two independent proofs: (a) via finite union of singletons, each of which is closed; (b) via the direct observation that $F$ has no limit points.

**Proof (a): Finite union of closed singletons.**

*Step 1.* Each singleton $\{a_i\}$ is closed. Indeed,
$$\mathbb{R} \setminus \{a_i\} = (-\infty, a_i) \cup (a_i, \infty),$$
a union of two open half-lines, which is open by Theorem 5.6(2). So $\{a_i\}$ is closed by Theorem 5.14.

*Step 2.* $F = \bigcup_{i=1}^n \{a_i\}$ is a finite union of closed sets, hence closed by Theorem 5.15(3).

**Proof (b): Direct, via limit points.**

*Claim.* $F' = \emptyset$.

Let $x \in \mathbb{R}$ be arbitrary. Define
$$\delta = \min\{|x - a_i| : a_i \neq x,\ i = 1, \ldots, n\} > 0,$$
or $\delta = 1$ if the set is empty (i.e., $F = \{x\}$ or $F = \emptyset$). (Minimum over a finite positive set is positive.)

Then $N_\delta(x) \cap F \subseteq \{x\}$: the only possible element of $F$ in $N_\delta(x)$ is $x$ itself. So the *deleted* neighbourhood $N_\delta^*(x) \cap F = \emptyset$, meaning $x$ is not a limit point of $F$.

Since $x$ was arbitrary, $F' = \emptyset$.

*Conclusion.* $\overline{F} = F \cup F' = F$, so $F$ is closed.

**Verification.** The two proofs agree. Method (a) uses the abstract topological axioms; method (b) uses the pointwise definition. Both are commonly used in qualifying exams.

**Interpretation.** Finiteness forces discreteness: any finite set of reals is "spread out" with no accumulation. This is a special case of the more general principle that any *discrete* set (every point isolated) with no cluster at infinity is closed. $\mathbb{Z}$ is another example. $\blacksquare$

**Solution 1(a).** $S = (0, 1] \cup \{2\}$.

*Setup.* Half-open interval with an attached isolated point.

*Strategy.* Analyse interior point-by-point, find limit points, compute closure and boundary.

*Interior:* Which points $x \in S$ have $N_\varepsilon(x) \subseteq S$ for some $\varepsilon > 0$?
- For $x \in (0, 1)$: take $\varepsilon = \min(x, 1 - x) > 0$; then $N_\varepsilon(x) \subseteq (0, 1) \subseteq S$. So $x$ is interior.
- For $x = 1$: every $N_\varepsilon(1) = (1 - \varepsilon, 1 + \varepsilon)$ contains $1 + \varepsilon/2 > 1$. We need $1 + \varepsilon/2 \in S$, but $1 + \varepsilon/2 \notin (0, 1]$ and $1 + \varepsilon/2 \neq 2$ for small $\varepsilon$. So $1$ is not interior.
- For $x = 2$: $N_\varepsilon(2) \cap S = \{2\}$ for $\varepsilon < 1$, so $N_\varepsilon(2) \not\subseteq S$ because $N_\varepsilon(2)$ contains $2 - \varepsilon/2 \notin S$. So $2$ is not interior.

Hence $\operatorname{int}(S) = (0, 1)$.

*Derived set $S'$:* 
- Every $x \in [0, 1]$ is a limit point: for any $\varepsilon > 0$, $N_\varepsilon(x)$ contains points of $(0, 1] \subseteq S$ other than $x$ (density).
- $2$ is isolated: $N_{1/2}(2) \cap S = \{2\}$, so $2$ is not a limit point.
- No point outside $[0, 1] \cup \{2\}$ is a limit point: if $x < 0$ or $x > 2$ or $x \in (1, 2)$, then $x$ has positive distance to $S$, giving a neighbourhood avoiding $S$.

So $S' = [0, 1]$.

*Closure:* $\overline{S} = S \cup S' = ((0, 1] \cup \{2\}) \cup [0, 1] = [0, 1] \cup \{2\}$.

*Boundary:* $\partial S = \overline{S} \setminus \operatorname{int}(S) = ([0, 1] \cup \{2\}) \setminus (0, 1) = \{0, 1, 2\}$.

*Status:* Not open ($1$ not interior). Not closed ($0 \in S'$ but $0 \notin S$).

*Interpretation.* The half-open endpoint $1 \in S$ fails to be interior; the missing endpoint $0 \notin S$ fails to be captured; and the isolated point $2$ is in the boundary because it is in the closure but not the interior. $\blacksquare$

**Solution 1(b).** $S = \{(-1)^n + 1/n : n \in \mathbb{N}\}$.

*Setup.* Listing elements: $n = 1$: $-1 + 1 = 0$. $n = 2$: $1 + 1/2 = 3/2$. $n = 3$: $-1 + 1/3 = -2/3$. $n = 4$: $1 + 1/4 = 5/4$. $n = 5$: $-1 + 1/5 = -4/5$. $\ldots$

The even-indexed subsequence $1 + 1/(2k)$ decreases to $1$; the odd-indexed subsequence $-1 + 1/(2k-1)$ decreases to $-1$.

*Strategy.* Identify the two accumulation points ($\pm 1$), verify each element is isolated.

*Interior:* Each element $s_n = (-1)^n + 1/n$ is isolated: $S$ is discrete except for accumulation at $\pm 1$. For any $n$, pick $\varepsilon > 0$ smaller than half the distance from $s_n$ to the nearest other element of $S$; then $N_\varepsilon(s_n) \cap S = \{s_n\}$, so $N_\varepsilon(s_n) \not\subseteq S$ (the neighbourhood contains non-$S$ points, e.g., $s_n + \varepsilon/2$). Hence no point is interior. $\operatorname{int}(S) = \emptyset$.

*Derived set $S'$:*
- $1 \in S'$: the subsequence $s_{2k} = 1 + 1/(2k) \to 1$ with $s_{2k} \neq 1$ (since $1/(2k) > 0$).
- $-1 \in S'$: the subsequence $s_{2k-1} = -1 + 1/(2k-1) \to -1$ with $s_{2k-1} \neq -1$.
- No other limit points: any $x \notin \{-1, 1\} \cup S$ has positive distance to $S$ (the set is discrete away from $\pm 1$).
- Each $s_n$ is not a limit point: isolated.

So $S' = \{-1, 1\}$.

*Closure:* $\overline{S} = S \cup \{-1, 1\}$.

*Boundary:* $\partial S = \overline{S} \setminus \operatorname{int}(S) = \overline{S}$ (since interior is empty).

*Status:* Not open ($\operatorname{int}(S) = \emptyset \neq S$). Not closed ($\pm 1 \in S' \setminus S$).

*Interpretation.* A doubly-oscillating convergent sequence: the "limits" are $\pm 1$, neither of which is an actual value of the sequence. Including them would produce a closed set. $\blacksquare$

**Solution 1(c).** $S = \mathbb{Q} \cap (0, 1)$.

*Setup.* Rationals strictly between $0$ and $1$.

*Strategy.* Density of $\mathbb{Q}$ and density of irrationals interplay.

*Interior:* Every $x \in S$ has irrational neighbours in $(0, 1) \setminus \mathbb{Q}$ arbitrarily close, so no neighbourhood lies in $S$. $\operatorname{int}(S) = \emptyset$.

*Derived set:* Every $x \in [0, 1]$ is a limit point. For $x \in (0, 1)$: by density of $\mathbb{Q}$, every neighbourhood of $x$ contains rationals other than $x$. For $x = 0$: rationals $1/n \in S$ approach $0$. For $x = 1$: rationals $1 - 1/n \in S$ approach $1$. No point outside $[0, 1]$ is a limit point. So $S' = [0, 1]$.

*Closure:* $\overline{S} = S \cup S' = [0, 1]$ (since $S \subseteq [0, 1]$).

*Boundary:* $\partial S = [0, 1] \setminus \emptyset = [0, 1]$.

*Status:* Not open. Not closed ($\overline{S} = [0, 1] \neq S$).

*Interpretation.* The rationals in an interval have maximal topological "fuzz": their closure is the whole interval, but they contribute nothing to the interior. This makes them boundary-heavy. $\blacksquare$

**Solution 1(d).** $S = \bigcup_{n=1}^\infty (n, n + 1)$.

*Setup.* Union of open intervals $(1, 2), (2, 3), (3, 4), \ldots$, covering $(1, \infty)$ except for integers $\geq 2$.

*Strategy.* Use Theorem 5.6(2) for openness, then identify which integers are limit points.

*Interior:* $S$ is a countable union of opens, hence open (Theorem 5.6(2)). So $\operatorname{int}(S) = S$.

*Derived set:*
- Every $x \in S$ is a limit point: pick $\varepsilon > 0$ small; $N_\varepsilon(x) \cap S$ contains infinitely many points of $S$.
- Every integer $n \geq 2$ is a limit point: approached from $(n-1, n)$ on the left and from $(n, n+1)$ on the right.
- $n = 1$ is a limit point: approached from the right via $(1, 2)$.
- No point $< 1$ is a limit point: has positive distance to $(1, \infty)$.

So $S' = [1, \infty)$.

*Closure:* $\overline{S} = S \cup S' = S \cup [1, \infty)$. Since $S \subseteq (1, \infty) \subseteq [1, \infty)$, this simplifies to $\overline{S} = [1, \infty)$.

*Boundary:* $\partial S = [1, \infty) \setminus S = \{1, 2, 3, 4, \ldots\} = \{n \in \mathbb{Z} : n \geq 1\}$.

*Status:* Open. Not closed (the positive integers $\geq 1$ are in $\overline{S}$ but not $S$).

*Interpretation.* The gaps at integers are the boundary: they are "sandwiched" between the adjacent open intervals, each of which touches them but does not include them. $\blacksquare$

**Solution 2.** Prove that $S \subseteq \mathbb{R}$ is open iff $S = \operatorname{int}(S)$.

*Setup.* This is Definition 5.4 unpacked, but worth writing out carefully.

*Strategy.* Both directions are direct unpackings of definitions.

*Proof.*

**($\Rightarrow$)** Assume $S$ is open. We show $S = \operatorname{int}(S)$, i.e., two inclusions.

$\operatorname{int}(S) \subseteq S$: by definition of interior, $\operatorname{int}(S) = \{x \in S : x \text{ is interior}\}$, so every element of $\operatorname{int}(S)$ is an element of $S$.

$S \subseteq \operatorname{int}(S)$: take $x \in S$. Since $S$ is open, by Definition 5.4 every point of $S$ is an interior point, so $x$ is an interior point, so $x \in \operatorname{int}(S)$.

Both inclusions give $S = \operatorname{int}(S)$.

**($\Leftarrow$)** Assume $S = \operatorname{int}(S)$. Take any $x \in S = \operatorname{int}(S)$. By definition of $\operatorname{int}(S)$, $x$ is an interior point of $S$. Since $x$ was arbitrary, every point of $S$ is an interior point, so $S$ is open.

$\blacksquare$

*Interpretation.* This shows that "open" and "equal to its own interior" are two equivalent formulations of the same property. Which is useful depends on context: "$S$ is open" is the atomic declarative; "$S = \operatorname{int}(S)$" is the equation-style assertion suited to manipulating via the operators $\operatorname{int}, \overline{\cdot}, \partial$.

**Solution 3.** $\mathbb{Z}$ has no limit points; $\mathbb{Z}$ is closed.

*Setup.* Prove $\mathbb{Z}' = \emptyset$ and conclude closedness.

*Strategy.* For each real number, find a neighbourhood containing at most one integer — which precludes it from being a limit point of $\mathbb{Z}$.

*Proof.*

**Step 1 ($\mathbb{Z}' = \emptyset$).** Take any $x \in \mathbb{R}$. We must show there is $\varepsilon > 0$ with $N_\varepsilon^*(x) \cap \mathbb{Z} = \emptyset$ (i.e., the deleted neighbourhood contains no integer).

*Case 1: $x \in \mathbb{Z}$.* Take $\varepsilon = 1/2$. Then $N_\varepsilon(x) = (x - 1/2, x + 1/2)$. The only integer in this interval is $x$ itself (the neighbouring integers $x - 1$ and $x + 1$ are at distance $1 > 1/2$). So $N_\varepsilon(x) \cap \mathbb{Z} = \{x\}$ and $N_\varepsilon^*(x) \cap \mathbb{Z} = \emptyset$.

*Case 2: $x \notin \mathbb{Z}$.* Let $n = \lfloor x \rfloor$ and $m = n + 1 = \lceil x \rceil$. Then $n < x < m$. Set
$$\varepsilon = \min(x - n, m - x) > 0.$$
For any integer $k$ with $|k - x| < \varepsilon$: if $k \leq n$, then $x - k \geq x - n \geq \varepsilon$, contradicting $|k - x| < \varepsilon$. If $k \geq m$, then $k - x \geq m - x \geq \varepsilon$, again a contradiction. So no integer is in $N_\varepsilon(x)$, and a fortiori none in $N_\varepsilon^*(x)$.

In both cases, $x \notin \mathbb{Z}'$. Since $x$ was arbitrary, $\mathbb{Z}' = \emptyset$.

**Step 2 ($\mathbb{Z}$ is closed).** Since $\mathbb{Z}' = \emptyset \subseteq \mathbb{Z}$, we have $\overline{\mathbb{Z}} = \mathbb{Z} \cup \mathbb{Z}' = \mathbb{Z}$. By Definition 5.13, $\mathbb{Z}$ is closed.

*Alternative verification.* $\mathbb{R} \setminus \mathbb{Z} = \bigcup_{n \in \mathbb{Z}}(n, n+1)$ is a union of open intervals, hence open (Theorem 5.6(2)). So $\mathbb{Z}$ is closed by Theorem 5.14.

$\blacksquare$

*Interpretation.* $\mathbb{Z}$ is a **discrete closed set**: all points isolated, no accumulation, but topologically closed (no limit points to miss). This is typical of any discrete set with no cluster at infinity — another example is $\{n + 1/n : n \in \mathbb{N}\}$.

**Solution 4.** $A$ open, $B$ closed $\Rightarrow A \setminus B$ open and $B \setminus A$ closed.

*Setup.* $A \setminus B = A \cap B^c$; $B \setminus A = B \cap A^c$.

*Strategy.* Open-closed duality: $B$ closed $\Leftrightarrow B^c$ open, and intersect with $A$ (open) or $B$ (closed) accordingly.

*Proof.*

**Part 1: $A \setminus B$ is open.**

$A \setminus B = A \cap B^c$.

By Theorem 5.14, $B$ closed implies $B^c$ open. So $A \setminus B$ is the intersection of two open sets $A$ and $B^c$. By Theorem 5.6(3) (finite intersection of opens is open, with $n = 2$), $A \setminus B$ is open.

**Part 2: $B \setminus A$ is closed.**

$B \setminus A = B \cap A^c$.

By Theorem 5.14, $A$ open implies $A^c$ closed. So $B \setminus A$ is the intersection of two closed sets $B$ and $A^c$. By Theorem 5.15(2) (arbitrary intersection of closeds is closed, in particular finite intersections), $B \setminus A$ is closed.

$\blacksquare$

*Interpretation.* "Subtracting" preserves the topological nature of the set being subtracted *from*, provided the set being subtracted has opposite topological type. Symbolically:

| $A$ \\ $B$ | $B$ open | $B$ closed |
|---|---|---|
| $A$ open | ? (can be either) | open |
| $A$ closed | closed | ? |

The "?" entries can be anything: e.g., $A = B = (0, 1)$ gives $A \setminus B = \emptyset$, both open and closed. The *guaranteed* cases are as in this problem.

**Solution 5.** $\overline{A \cup B} = \overline{A} \cup \overline{B}$ and $\overline{A \cap B} \subseteq \overline{A} \cap \overline{B}$ with possible strict inclusion.

This is Proposition 5.12 parts (5)–(6); we redo it here using the sequential characterisation for variety.

*Setup.* Use Theorem 5.16: $x \in \overline{E} \iff \exists$ sequence in $E$ converging to $x$.

*Strategy.* Apply sequential definitions and pigeonhole.

**Part 1: $\overline{A \cup B} = \overline{A} \cup \overline{B}$.**

**($\supseteq$)** $A \subseteq A \cup B$, so by monotonicity (Proposition 5.12(4)) $\overline{A} \subseteq \overline{A \cup B}$. Similarly $\overline{B} \subseteq \overline{A \cup B}$. Union gives $\overline{A} \cup \overline{B} \subseteq \overline{A \cup B}$.

**($\subseteq$)** Take $x \in \overline{A \cup B}$. By Theorem 5.16, there is a sequence $(x_n) \subseteq A \cup B$ with $x_n \to x$.

By the pigeonhole principle, at least one of the sets
$$I_A = \{n : x_n \in A\}, \qquad I_B = \{n : x_n \in B\}$$
is infinite (their union is $\mathbb{N}$).

Say $I_A$ is infinite. Then the subsequence $(x_n)_{n \in I_A}$ is contained in $A$. A subsequence of a convergent sequence has the same limit, so $x_n \to x$ along $I_A$, giving $x \in \overline{A}$ (Theorem 5.16). Hence $x \in \overline{A} \cup \overline{B}$.

Similarly if $I_B$ is infinite.

**Part 2: $\overline{A \cap B} \subseteq \overline{A} \cap \overline{B}$ with possibly strict inclusion.**

$A \cap B \subseteq A$, so $\overline{A \cap B} \subseteq \overline{A}$ by monotonicity. Similarly $\overline{A \cap B} \subseteq \overline{B}$. Intersecting: $\overline{A \cap B} \subseteq \overline{A} \cap \overline{B}$.

*Counterexample for strict inclusion.* Let $A = \mathbb{Q}$ and $B = \mathbb{R} \setminus \mathbb{Q}$. Then:
- $A \cap B = \emptyset$, so $\overline{A \cap B} = \overline{\emptyset} = \emptyset$.
- $\overline{A} = \overline{\mathbb{Q}} = \mathbb{R}$ (density of rationals).
- $\overline{B} = \overline{\mathbb{R} \setminus \mathbb{Q}} = \mathbb{R}$ (density of irrationals).
- $\overline{A} \cap \overline{B} = \mathbb{R} \cap \mathbb{R} = \mathbb{R}$.

So $\overline{A \cap B} = \emptyset \subsetneq \mathbb{R} = \overline{A} \cap \overline{B}$ — maximal possible strict inclusion. $\blacksquare$

*Interpretation.* Closure distributes over finite unions but only sub-distributes over intersections. This asymmetry traces back to the universal/existential quantifier structure of the definitions: adherence is an *existential* condition, which behaves well under unions but badly under intersections. The dual phenomenon holds for interior: $\operatorname{int}(A \cap B) = \operatorname{int}(A) \cap \operatorname{int}(B)$ but $\operatorname{int}(A \cup B) \supseteq \operatorname{int}(A) \cup \operatorname{int}(B)$ with possible strict inclusion.

**Solution 6.** $\overline{\operatorname{int}(S)} \neq S$ for some closed $S$.

*Setup.* We want a closed set $S$ (so $\overline{S} = S$) such that $\overline{\operatorname{int}(S)} \subsetneq S$.

*Strategy.* Pick a closed set with empty interior but non-trivial content — e.g., the Cantor set, or simpler, a finite set.

*Example 1: $S = \{0\}$.* Interior: $\operatorname{int}(\{0\}) = \emptyset$ (singletons in $\mathbb{R}$ are not open). Closure of interior: $\overline{\emptyset} = \emptyset$. But $S = \{0\} \neq \emptyset$. So $\overline{\operatorname{int}(S)} \subsetneq S$.

*Example 2: $S = \{1/n : n \in \mathbb{N}\} \cup \{0\}$ (from Example 1 of worked problems).* We showed $S$ is closed with $\operatorname{int}(S) = \emptyset$. So $\overline{\operatorname{int}(S)} = \emptyset \neq S$.

*Example 3: The Cantor set $C$.* Closed (Example 3), and $\operatorname{int}(C) = \emptyset$ (Cantor set contains no open interval since at each stage we remove open middles). So $\overline{\operatorname{int}(C)} = \emptyset \subsetneq C$.

*Interpretation.* A closed set $S$ satisfies $\overline{\operatorname{int}(S)} = S$ precisely when $S$ is the closure of an open set, i.e., $S$ has "empty filling" or equals the closure of its own interior. Such sets are called **regular closed sets**. Non-regular closed sets (like finite sets, discrete sets, or the Cantor set) have "thin" structure that the interior cannot recover. This is the starting point for the theory of regular open sets and the Boolean algebra $RO(\mathbb{R})$ of regular open sets. $\blacksquare$

**Solution 7.** Every closed $F \subseteq \mathbb{R}$ is a countable intersection of open sets (a $G_\delta$).

*Setup.* Want to write $F = \bigcap_{n=1}^\infty U_n$ with each $U_n$ open.

*Strategy.* Inflate $F$ slightly on each scale $1/n$ using the $1/n$-neighbourhood of $F$:
$$U_n = \bigcup_{x \in F} N_{1/n}(x) = \{y \in \mathbb{R} : \operatorname{dist}(y, F) < 1/n\}.$$

*Proof.*

**Step 1: $U_n$ is open.** $U_n = \bigcup_{x \in F} N_{1/n}(x)$ is a union of open intervals, hence open by Theorem 5.6(2). (Alternative: $U_n = d_F^{-1}([0, 1/n))$ where $d_F(y) = \inf_{x \in F}|y - x|$ is a continuous function; the preimage of the open set $[0, 1/n)$ is open.)

**Step 2: $F \subseteq \bigcap_n U_n$.** For every $x \in F$ and every $n$, $x \in N_{1/n}(x) \subseteq U_n$. So $x \in U_n$ for all $n$, hence $x \in \bigcap U_n$.

**Step 3: $\bigcap_n U_n \subseteq F$.** Take $y \in \bigcap_n U_n$. For each $n$, $y \in U_n$, so there exists $x_n \in F$ with $|y - x_n| < 1/n$. 

This gives a sequence $(x_n) \subseteq F$ with $|x_n - y| < 1/n \to 0$, so $x_n \to y$. 

Since $F$ is closed, by Theorem 5.17, $y = \lim x_n \in F$.

**Conclusion.** $F = \bigcap_{n=1}^\infty U_n$, a countable intersection of open sets. $\blacksquare$

*Interpretation.* This exhibits every closed set as a $G_\delta$. By complementation, every open set is an $F_\sigma$ (countable union of closed sets). The hierarchy $G_\delta, F_\sigma, G_{\delta\sigma}, F_{\sigma\delta}, \ldots$ continues into the **Borel hierarchy**, a central construction in descriptive set theory. On $\mathbb{R}$: every Borel set sits at some finite level, and the levels are all distinct.
