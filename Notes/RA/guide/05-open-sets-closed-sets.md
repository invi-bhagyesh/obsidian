# 5. Open Sets and Closed Sets in $\mathbb{R}$

---

## 5.1 Topology on the Real Line

Open and closed sets are the *shape* language of analysis. They capture "which points are genuinely interior" and "which are genuinely on the boundary," letting us define continuity, compactness, and convergence in intrinsic terms.

On $\mathbb{R}$ the whole structure is generated by **open intervals** $(a, b)$, which play the role of "nearby neighbourhoods."

---

## 5.2 Neighbourhoods and Interior Points

### $\varepsilon$-neighbourhood

> **Definition.** For $x_0 \in \mathbb{R}$ and $\varepsilon > 0$, the **$\varepsilon$-neighbourhood** of $x_0$ is
> $$N_\varepsilon(x_0) = (x_0 - \varepsilon,\ x_0 + \varepsilon) = \{x \in \mathbb{R} : |x - x_0| < \varepsilon\}.$$

We also use the term **deleted neighbourhood**:
$$N_\varepsilon^*(x_0) = N_\varepsilon(x_0) \setminus \{x_0\} = \{x : 0 < |x - x_0| < \varepsilon\}.$$

### Interior points

> **Definition.** Let $S \subseteq \mathbb{R}$. A point $x \in S$ is an **interior point** of $S$ if there exists $\varepsilon > 0$ with $N_\varepsilon(x) \subseteq S$.

Intuitively: $x$ is interior if it has a whole neighbourhood sitting inside $S$, so $x$ is "thick inside" $S$.

> **Definition.** The **interior** of $S$ is
> $$\operatorname{int}(S) = S^\circ = \{x \in S : x \text{ is an interior point of } S\}.$$

### Examples
- $S = [0, 1]$: $\operatorname{int}(S) = (0, 1)$. $0$ and $1$ are not interior ($0$'s every neighbourhood contains points $< 0$).
- $S = \mathbb{Q}$: $\operatorname{int}(\mathbb{Q}) = \emptyset$. Every rational has irrationals arbitrarily close.
- $S = (0, 1) \cup \{2\}$: $\operatorname{int}(S) = (0, 1)$; $2$ is an isolated point, not interior.

---

## 5.3 Open Sets

### Definition and basic properties

> **Definition.** $S \subseteq \mathbb{R}$ is **open** if every point of $S$ is an interior point. Equivalently, $S = \operatorname{int}(S)$.

**Convention:** the empty set $\emptyset$ is open (vacuously).

### Examples
- Every open interval $(a, b)$ is open. *Proof:* for $x \in (a, b)$, take $\varepsilon = \min(x - a, b - x) > 0$; then $N_\varepsilon(x) \subseteq (a, b)$. $\blacksquare$
- $\mathbb{R}$ is open.
- $(-\infty, a)$, $(a, \infty)$ are open.
- $\emptyset$ is open.
- $[a, b]$ is **not** open: $a$ is not interior.
- $\mathbb{Q}$ is **not** open: no rational is interior.

### Fundamental theorem on open sets

> **Theorem.** The class of open subsets of $\mathbb{R}$ satisfies:
> 1. $\emptyset$ and $\mathbb{R}$ are open.
> 2. The union of any family of open sets is open.
> 3. The intersection of *finitely many* open sets is open.

*Proof.* (2) Let $\{U_\alpha\}_{\alpha \in I}$ be open. Take $x \in \bigcup_\alpha U_\alpha$. Then $x \in U_{\alpha_0}$ for some $\alpha_0$. Since $U_{\alpha_0}$ is open, $\exists \varepsilon > 0$ with $N_\varepsilon(x) \subseteq U_{\alpha_0} \subseteq \bigcup U_\alpha$.

(3) Let $U_1, \ldots, U_n$ be open. Take $x \in \bigcap U_i$. For each $i$, $\exists \varepsilon_i > 0$ with $N_{\varepsilon_i}(x) \subseteq U_i$. Let $\varepsilon = \min(\varepsilon_1, \ldots, \varepsilon_n) > 0$. Then $N_\varepsilon(x) \subseteq U_i$ for all $i$, so $N_\varepsilon(x) \subseteq \bigcap U_i$. $\blacksquare$

**Warning:** "finitely many" in (3) is essential. $\bigcap_{n=1}^\infty (-1/n, 1/n) = \{0\}$, which is not open.

### Structure theorem

> **Theorem.** Every non-empty open subset of $\mathbb{R}$ is a countable disjoint union of open intervals.

*Proof sketch.* For each $x \in U$, let $I_x$ = largest open interval containing $x$ that is contained in $U$ (union of all such intervals). Two $I_x, I_y$ are either equal or disjoint. Each component $I_x$ contains a rational, and distinct components give distinct rationals, so the number of components is at most $|\mathbb{Q}| = \aleph_0$. $\blacksquare$

## 5.4 Limit Points and Adherent Points

### Limit points

> **Definition.** Let $S \subseteq \mathbb{R}$. A point $x \in \mathbb{R}$ (not necessarily in $S$) is a **limit point** (or **accumulation point** or **cluster point**) of $S$ if every $\varepsilon$-neighbourhood of $x$ contains a point of $S$ other than $x$:
> $$\forall \varepsilon > 0,\ \exists s \in S \text{ with } 0 < |s - x| < \varepsilon.$$

Equivalently: every deleted neighbourhood of $x$ meets $S$.

### Adherent points (closure points)

> **Definition.** $x \in \mathbb{R}$ is an **adherent point** of $S$ if every $\varepsilon$-neighbourhood of $x$ contains a point of $S$:
> $$\forall \varepsilon > 0,\ \exists s \in S \text{ with } |s - x| < \varepsilon.$$

Every element of $S$ is an adherent point (take $s = x$). Every limit point is adherent (a neighbourhood containing some $s \neq x$ in $S$ contains a point of $S$).

### Isolated points

> **Definition.** $x \in S$ is an **isolated point** of $S$ if some $\varepsilon$-neighbourhood of $x$ meets $S$ only at $x$:
> $$\exists \varepsilon > 0,\ N_\varepsilon(x) \cap S = \{x\}.$$

Every point of $S$ is either a limit point of $S$ or an isolated point (exhaustive, exclusive).

### Examples
- $S = (0, 1)$: limit points = $[0, 1]$ (including $0$ and $1$, which are not in $S$). Adherent points = $[0, 1]$. No isolated points.
- $S = \{1/n : n \in \mathbb{N}\}$: $0$ is a limit point (not in $S$). Every $1/n$ is isolated. Adherent points = $S \cup \{0\}$.
- $S = \mathbb{Z}$: limit points = $\emptyset$ (no accumulation). Every integer is isolated. Adherent points = $\mathbb{Z}$.
- $S = \mathbb{Q}$: limit points = $\mathbb{R}$ (density). Adherent points = $\mathbb{R}$. No isolated points.

---

## 5.5 Closure of a Set

> **Definition.** The **closure** of $S$ is the set of all adherent points:
> $$\overline{S} = \{x \in \mathbb{R} : \forall \varepsilon > 0,\ N_\varepsilon(x) \cap S \neq \emptyset\}.$$

Equivalently, $\overline{S} = S \cup S'$, where $S'$ = set of limit points of $S$ (called the **derived set**).

### Properties

> **Proposition.**
> 1. $S \subseteq \overline{S}$.
> 2. $\overline{\overline{S}} = \overline{S}$.
> 3. $A \subseteq B \Rightarrow \overline{A} \subseteq \overline{B}$.
> 4. $\overline{A \cup B} = \overline{A} \cup \overline{B}$.
> 5. $\overline{A \cap B} \subseteq \overline{A} \cap \overline{B}$ (strict inclusion possible).

**Example of strict inclusion in (5):** $A = \mathbb{Q}$, $B = \mathbb{R} \setminus \mathbb{Q}$. $A \cap B = \emptyset$, so $\overline{A \cap B} = \emptyset$; but $\overline{A} = \overline{B} = \mathbb{R}$, intersecting to $\mathbb{R}$.

---

## 5.6 Closed Sets

### Definition

> **Definition.** $S \subseteq \mathbb{R}$ is **closed** if it contains all its limit points â€” equivalently, $S = \overline{S}$.

### Relationship with open sets

> **Theorem.** $S$ is closed $\iff$ its complement $\mathbb{R} \setminus S$ is open.

*Proof.* ($\Rightarrow$) Suppose $S$ is closed. Take $x \notin S$. If $x$ were a limit point of $S$, then $x \in S$ (closed); so $x$ is not a limit point. Then some $\varepsilon$-neighbourhood of $x$ has $N_\varepsilon(x) \cap S = \emptyset$ or $\{x\}$; since $x \notin S$, $N_\varepsilon(x) \cap S = \emptyset$, i.e., $N_\varepsilon(x) \subseteq \mathbb{R} \setminus S$. So $\mathbb{R} \setminus S$ is open.

($\Leftarrow$) Suppose $\mathbb{R} \setminus S$ is open. Let $x$ be a limit point of $S$. If $x \notin S$, then $x \in \mathbb{R} \setminus S$; openness gives $\varepsilon > 0$ with $N_\varepsilon(x) \subseteq \mathbb{R} \setminus S$, i.e., $N_\varepsilon(x) \cap S = \emptyset$, contradicting that $x$ is a limit point. So $x \in S$, and $S$ is closed. $\blacksquare$

### Examples
- $[a, b]$ is closed. Complement $(-\infty, a) \cup (b, \infty)$ is open (union of opens).
- $[a, \infty)$, $(-\infty, b]$ are closed.
- $\{x_0\}$ is closed.
- $\mathbb{Z}$ is closed (no limit points).
- $\emptyset$ and $\mathbb{R}$ are both open **and** closed.
- $\mathbb{Q}$ is neither open nor closed.
- $\{1/n : n \in \mathbb{N}\}$ is neither open nor closed ($0$ is a missing limit point).
- $\{0\} \cup \{1/n : n \in \mathbb{N}\}$ is closed.

### Dual theorem

> **Theorem.** The class of closed subsets of $\mathbb{R}$ satisfies:
> 1. $\emptyset$ and $\mathbb{R}$ are closed.
> 2. The intersection of any family of closed sets is closed.
> 3. The union of finitely many closed sets is closed.

*Proof.* De Morgan applied to Â§5.3 Theorem: $\mathbb{R} \setminus \bigcap C_\alpha = \bigcup (\mathbb{R} \setminus C_\alpha)$, union of opens = open, so intersection of closeds = closed. Etc. $\blacksquare$

## 5.7 Characterisations via Sequences

> **Theorem.** $S \subseteq \mathbb{R}$ is closed $\iff$ for every convergent sequence $(x_n)$ with $x_n \in S$ for all $n$, the limit $\lim x_n \in S$.

This "sequences stay inside" characterisation is the workhorse once we have sequences in hand (see [[08-sequences-introduction]]).

> **Theorem.** $x \in \overline{S}$ $\iff$ there exists a sequence $(x_n)$ in $S$ with $x_n \to x$.

*Proof.* ($\Rightarrow$) For each $n$, by adherence $\exists x_n \in S$ with $|x_n - x| < 1/n$. Then $x_n \to x$.

($\Leftarrow$) If $x_n \to x$ with $x_n \in S$, every $\varepsilon$-neighbourhood of $x$ contains some $x_n$ (take $n$ large enough), so $x$ is adherent. $\blacksquare$

These characterisations make "closed" and "sequential closure" coincide on $\mathbb{R}$.

---

## 5.8 Boundary of a Set

> **Definition.** The **boundary** of $S$ is $\partial S = \overline{S} \cap \overline{\mathbb{R} \setminus S}$.

A point is in the boundary iff every neighbourhood meets both $S$ and its complement. Equivalently, $\partial S = \overline{S} \setminus \operatorname{int}(S)$.

### Examples
- $\partial[0, 1] = \partial(0, 1) = \{0, 1\}$.
- $\partial \mathbb{Q} = \mathbb{R}$ (every real is on the boundary of $\mathbb{Q}$).
- $\partial \mathbb{Z} = \mathbb{Z}$.

---

## 5.9 Dense Sets

> **Definition.** $D \subseteq \mathbb{R}$ is **dense in $\mathbb{R}$** if $\overline{D} = \mathbb{R}$. Equivalently, every non-empty open interval contains a point of $D$.

Classic examples:
- $\mathbb{Q}$ is dense in $\mathbb{R}$.
- $\mathbb{R} \setminus \mathbb{Q}$ is dense in $\mathbb{R}$.
- The dyadic rationals $\{m/2^n : m \in \mathbb{Z}, n \in \mathbb{N}\}$ are dense.

Density is what allows $\mathbb{Q}$-valued information (which is countable) to determine $\mathbb{R}$-valued functions, a central theme in approximation theory.

## Worked Examples

**Example 1:** Determine whether $S = \{1/n : n \in \mathbb{N}\} \cup \{0\}$ is open, closed, or neither. Find $\operatorname{int}(S)$, $S'$ (limit points), $\overline{S}$, $\partial S$.

*Solution:*
- $\operatorname{int}(S) = \emptyset$: every point $1/n$ has irrational neighbours outside $S$; for $0$, same story.
- $S' = \{0\}$: $0$ is the only limit point (sequence $1/n \to 0$). Each $1/n$ is isolated.
- $\overline{S} = S \cup S' = S$. So $S$ is closed.
- $\partial S = \overline{S} \setminus \operatorname{int}(S) = S$.

Status: closed, not open.

---

**Example 2:** Let $A = (0, 1) \cup (2, 3)$. Find $\operatorname{int}(A)$, $A'$, $\overline{A}$, $\partial A$. Is $A$ open? Closed?

*Solution:*
- Open sets are unions of open intervals; $A$ is a union of two open intervals, so $A$ is open. $\operatorname{int}(A) = A$.
- $A' = [0,1] \cup [2,3]$ (limit points include the endpoints).
- $\overline{A} = [0,1] \cup [2,3]$. Closed? $\overline{A} \neq A$, so $A$ is not closed.
- $\partial A = \{0, 1, 2, 3\}$.

---

**Example 3:** Prove that the Cantor set $C$ (usual middle-third construction) is closed.

*Solution:* $C = \bigcap_{n=0}^{\infty} C_n$ where each $C_n$ is a finite union of closed intervals (hence closed). Intersection of closed sets is closed (Â§5.6 dual theorem). $\blacksquare$

---

**Example 4:** Show that $\{x \in \mathbb{R} : \sin x > 0\}$ is open but not closed.

*Solution:* The set equals $\bigcup_{k \in \mathbb{Z}} (2k\pi,\ (2k+1)\pi)$, a countable union of open intervals, hence open.

It is not closed: $\pi \notin S$ but $\pi$ is a limit point (approach from the left). $\blacksquare$

---

**Example 5:** Prove that every finite subset $F = \{a_1, \ldots, a_n\}$ of $\mathbb{R}$ is closed.

*Solution:* Each $\{a_i\}$ is closed (its complement $(-\infty, a_i) \cup (a_i, \infty)$ is open). Finite union of closed sets is closed (Â§5.6 dual theorem). Alternatively, $F$ has no limit points, so $F = \overline{F}$. $\blacksquare$

## Practice Problems

1. Determine the interior, limit points, closure, boundary, and openness/closedness of:
   - (a) $S = (0, 1] \cup \{2\}$
   - (b) $S = \{(-1)^n + 1/n : n \in \mathbb{N}\}$
   - (c) $S = \mathbb{Q} \cap (0, 1)$
   - (d) $S = \bigcup_{n=1}^{\infty}(n, n+1)$

2. Prove that a set $S \subseteq \mathbb{R}$ is open iff $S = \operatorname{int}(S)$.

3. Show that $\mathbb{Z}$ has no limit points but is closed.

4. Prove: if $A$ is open and $B$ is closed, then $A \setminus B$ is open and $B \setminus A$ is closed.

5. Prove that $\overline{A \cup B} = \overline{A} \cup \overline{B}$ but $\overline{A \cap B} \subseteq \overline{A} \cap \overline{B}$ with possible strict inclusion.

### Solutions

**1(a).** $S = (0, 1] \cup \{2\}$.
- $\operatorname{int}(S) = (0, 1)$. ($1$ has points $> 1$ in any neighbourhood that are outside $S$. $2$ is isolated.)
- $S' = [0, 1]$. (Points in $(0,1)$ are limit points; $0$ is a limit point from the right; $1$ is a limit point from the left; $2$ is isolated â€” not a limit point.)
- $\overline{S} = [0, 1] \cup \{2\}$.
- $\partial S = \{0, 1, 2\}$.
- Not open (1 not interior). Not closed ($0 \notin S$ but is a limit point).

**1(b).** Elements: $0, 3/2, -2/3, 5/4, -4/5, \ldots$. As $n \to \infty$ along even subsequence $\to 1$; along odd $\to -1$.
- $\operatorname{int}(S) = \emptyset$.
- $S' = \{-1, 1\}$. Every point of $S$ is isolated.
- $\overline{S} = S \cup \{-1, 1\}$.
- $\partial S = \overline{S}$.
- Not open, not closed.

**1(c).** $S = \mathbb{Q} \cap (0, 1)$.
- $\operatorname{int}(S) = \emptyset$ (irrational neighbours).
- $S' = [0, 1]$ (density of $\mathbb{Q}$).
- $\overline{S} = [0, 1]$.
- $\partial S = [0, 1]$.
- Neither open nor closed.

**1(d).** $S = \bigcup_n (n, n+1)$.
- Open (union of opens). $\operatorname{int}(S) = S$.
- $S' = [1, \infty)$ (each $n$ is a limit point from either side, plus all interior points of intervals).
- $\overline{S} = [1, \infty)$.
- $\partial S = \mathbb{Z} \cap [1, \infty) = \{1, 2, 3, \ldots\}$.
- Open; not closed.

**2.** ($\Rightarrow$) If $S$ is open, every point of $S$ is interior, so $S \subseteq \operatorname{int}(S)$; the reverse $\operatorname{int}(S) \subseteq S$ is by definition. Hence $S = \operatorname{int}(S)$.

($\Leftarrow$) If $S = \operatorname{int}(S)$, every point of $S$ is interior, i.e., $S$ is open. $\blacksquare$

**3.** For any $n \in \mathbb{Z}$, the interval $(n - 1/2, n + 1/2)$ meets $\mathbb{Z}$ only at $n$. So no integer is a limit point of $\mathbb{Z}$, and $\mathbb{Z}' = \emptyset$. Hence $\overline{\mathbb{Z}} = \mathbb{Z} \cup \emptyset = \mathbb{Z}$, so $\mathbb{Z}$ is closed. $\blacksquare$

**4.** $A \setminus B = A \cap (\mathbb{R} \setminus B)$. $B$ closed $\Rightarrow \mathbb{R} \setminus B$ open. Intersection of two opens = open. So $A \setminus B$ is open.

$B \setminus A = B \cap (\mathbb{R} \setminus A)$. $A$ open $\Rightarrow \mathbb{R} \setminus A$ closed. Intersection of two closeds = closed. So $B \setminus A$ is closed. $\blacksquare$

**5.** Use the sequence characterisation (Â§5.7): $x \in \overline{E} \iff \exists$ sequence in $E$ converging to $x$.

$\overline{A \cup B}$: $x$ limit of $x_n \in A \cup B$. Infinitely many $x_n$ lie in $A$ or in $B$ (pigeonhole), giving a subsequence in one of them, so $x \in \overline{A} \cup \overline{B}$. Converse: $\overline{A} \cup \overline{B} \subseteq \overline{A \cup B}$ since $A \subseteq A \cup B$ (monotonicity of closure).

$\overline{A \cap B} \subseteq \overline{A} \cap \overline{B}$ by monotonicity.

Strict inclusion example: $A = \mathbb{Q}$, $B = \mathbb{R} \setminus \mathbb{Q}$. Then $A \cap B = \emptyset$ so $\overline{A \cap B} = \emptyset$; but $\overline{A} \cap \overline{B} = \mathbb{R}$. $\blacksquare$
