# 2. Symmetries of the Two-Dimensional Plane

> **Why this chapter.** Before the abstract definition of a group (file 03), we motivate the concept through **symmetries**: transformations of the plane that preserve distances. The symmetries of a geometric figure form the prototypical non-commutative group, and every concept in group theory — subgroup, order, generator, conjugation — has a geometric counterpart that is easiest to see in $\mathbb{R}^2$.

---

## 2.1 Isometries of the Plane

> **Definition (Isometry / rigid motion).** A function $T : \mathbb{R}^2 \to \mathbb{R}^2$ is an **isometry** (or **rigid motion**) if it preserves Euclidean distance:
> $$\|T(\vec{p}) - T(\vec{q})\| = \|\vec{p} - \vec{q}\| \quad \forall \vec{p}, \vec{q} \in \mathbb{R}^2.$$

Isometries are the "rigid" transformations — they preserve lengths and angles, and hence the shape of any figure.

> **Theorem 2.1 (Classification of plane isometries).** Every isometry $T : \mathbb{R}^2 \to \mathbb{R}^2$ is one of:
> 1. **Translation** $T_{\vec{v}}(\vec{p}) = \vec{p} + \vec{v}$.
> 2. **Rotation** $R_{\theta, \vec{c}}$ by angle $\theta$ about a point $\vec{c}$.
> 3. **Reflection** $F_\ell$ across a line $\ell$.
> 4. **Glide reflection** — a reflection followed by a translation parallel to the reflecting line.

*(Proof sketch: any isometry is determined by the images of three non-collinear points; a direct check shows the four types above exhaust the possibilities.)*

Isometries form a group under composition: the **Euclidean group** $E(2)$.

---

## 2.2 Symmetries of a Figure

> **Definition.** A **symmetry** of a set $F \subset \mathbb{R}^2$ is an isometry $T$ with $T(F) = F$ — i.e., $T$ maps $F$ onto itself (as a set).

The set of all symmetries of $F$, denoted $\text{Sym}(F)$, is closed under composition and inversion, so it forms a group (a subgroup of $E(2)$). We make this precise in [[03-groups-definition-and-examples]].

---

## 2.3 Symmetries of the Equilateral Triangle

Let $F$ be the equilateral triangle with vertices at $A = (1, 0)$, $B = (-1/2, \sqrt{3}/2)$, $C = (-1/2, -\sqrt{3}/2)$ (centered at origin).

**Rotation symmetries.** Rotations about the origin by $0°, 120°, 240°$ preserve $F$:
- $r_0 = \text{identity}$
- $r_1 = R_{120°}$: $A \to B \to C \to A$
- $r_2 = R_{240°}$: $A \to C \to B \to A$

**Reflection symmetries.** Reflections across the three lines through the center and a vertex:
- $s_A$ = reflection across the line through $A$ (fixes $A$, swaps $B \leftrightarrow C$)
- $s_B$ = reflection across the line through $B$ (fixes $B$, swaps $A \leftrightarrow C$)
- $s_C$ = reflection across the line through $C$ (fixes $C$, swaps $A \leftrightarrow B$)

**Total:** 6 symmetries — 3 rotations + 3 reflections. This is the **dihedral group $D_3$** (sometimes written $D_6$).

### Composition table for $D_3$

Using the convention **$fg$ means "apply $g$ first, then $f$"**:

$$
\begin{array}{c|cccccc}
\circ & r_0 & r_1 & r_2 & s_A & s_B & s_C \\
\hline
r_0 & r_0 & r_1 & r_2 & s_A & s_B & s_C \\
r_1 & r_1 & r_2 & r_0 & s_B & s_C & s_A \\
r_2 & r_2 & r_0 & r_1 & s_C & s_A & s_B \\
s_A & s_A & s_C & s_B & r_0 & r_2 & r_1 \\
s_B & s_B & s_A & s_C & r_1 & r_0 & r_2 \\
s_C & s_C & s_B & s_A & r_2 & r_1 & r_0
\end{array}
$$

**Key observations:**
- $r_1 r_1 = r_2$, $r_1 r_2 = r_0$, so rotations form a cyclic subgroup $\{r_0, r_1, r_2\}$ of order 3.
- $s_A s_A = s_B s_B = s_C s_C = r_0$, so each reflection is self-inverse.
- $s_A r_1 = s_C$ but $r_1 s_A = s_B$. **Non-commutative.** First non-abelian group!

## 2.4 Symmetries of the Square

Let $F$ be the unit square with vertices $\{(\pm 1, \pm 1)\}$.

**Rotation symmetries:** $R_0, R_{90°}, R_{180°}, R_{270°}$ — 4 rotations.

**Reflection symmetries:**
- $h$: reflection across horizontal axis (x-axis)
- $v$: reflection across vertical axis (y-axis)
- $d_1$: reflection across $y = x$ (main diagonal)
- $d_2$: reflection across $y = -x$ (anti-diagonal)

**Total:** 8 symmetries — the **dihedral group $D_4$**.

### Cayley diagram (schematic)

If $r = R_{90°}$ and $s = h$ (horizontal reflection), the 8 elements are:
$$D_4 = \{1, r, r^2, r^3, s, sr, sr^2, sr^3\}.$$

Key relation: $sr = r^{-1}s = r^3 s$, or equivalently $rs = sr^{-1}$. The group is generated by $\{r, s\}$ with relations:
$$r^4 = 1, \quad s^2 = 1, \quad srs = r^{-1}.$$
This is the **dihedral presentation**: a recipe encoding the whole group in 3 relations. More on this in [[04-subgroups-generators-cayley-diagrams]] and [[05-permutation-and-dihedral-groups]].

---

## 2.5 The Dihedral Group $D_n$

Generalizing, the symmetries of a regular $n$-gon form the **dihedral group $D_n$**, with $2n$ elements:
- $n$ rotations: $1, r, r^2, \ldots, r^{n-1}$ (where $r = R_{2\pi/n}$)
- $n$ reflections: $s, sr, sr^2, \ldots, sr^{n-1}$

**Presentation:**
$$D_n = \langle r, s \mid r^n = 1, s^2 = 1, srs = r^{-1} \rangle.$$

**Orders:**
- $|D_3| = 6$ (equilateral triangle)
- $|D_4| = 8$ (square)
- $|D_5| = 10$ (regular pentagon)
- $|D_6| = 12$ (regular hexagon)

> **Note on notation.** Some books write $D_{2n}$ instead of $D_n$ (emphasizing the order $2n$). This guide uses $D_n$ for the group of $2n$ elements (symmetries of the $n$-gon), matching [[05-permutation-and-dihedral-groups]] and the course plan for MAT 2235.

---

## 2.6 Rotation-Only Symmetries: The Cyclic Groups

If we restrict to **orientation-preserving** symmetries (rotations only, no reflections), we get the **cyclic group $C_n$ = $\{1, r, r^2, \ldots, r^{n-1}\}$** with $|C_n| = n$.

$C_n$ is a subgroup of $D_n$. The rotation symmetries of the pinwheel / propeller with $n$ blades (and no mirror symmetry) realize $C_n$.

## 2.7 Worked Examples

**Example 1 (Computing in $D_3$).** Compute $s_A r_1 s_A$ in the triangle group.

*Solution.* Start with vertex $A$, trace through each symmetry:
- Apply $s_A$ first: $A \to A$ ($A$ fixed), $B \to C$, $C \to B$.
- Then $r_1$ (rotate $120°$): $A \to B$, $C \to A$, $B \to C$. So after $r_1 \circ s_A$: $A \to B$, $B \to A$, $C \to C$. Wait — let me re-verify.

Applying $r_1 \circ s_A$ to $A$: first $s_A(A) = A$, then $r_1(A) = B$. So $(r_1 s_A)(A) = B$. 
To $B$: $s_A(B) = C$, $r_1(C) = A$. So $(r_1 s_A)(B) = A$. 
To $C$: $s_A(C) = B$, $r_1(B) = C$. So $(r_1 s_A)(C) = C$. 
Result: $A \leftrightarrow B$ swapped, $C$ fixed. That's $s_C$.

Now $s_A (r_1 s_A) = s_A \circ s_C$: $(s_A \circ s_C)(A) = s_A(s_C(A)) = s_A(B) = C$, $(s_A \circ s_C)(B) = s_A(A) = A$, $(s_A \circ s_C)(C) = s_A(C) = B$. So $A \to C \to B \to A$, which is $r_2$.

Hence $s_A r_1 s_A = r_2 = r_1^{-1}$. This confirms the key dihedral relation $s r s^{-1} = r^{-1}$. $\blacksquare$

**Example 2 (Inverse in $D_4$).** In $D_4 = \langle r, s \mid r^4 = s^2 = 1, srs = r^{-1} \rangle$, find $(rs)^{-1}$.

*Solution.* $(rs)^{-1} = s^{-1} r^{-1} = s r^{-1} = s r^3$. Verify: $(rs)(sr^3) = r(ss)r^3 = r \cdot 1 \cdot r^3 = r^4 = 1$. ✓ So $(rs)^{-1} = sr^3$. $\blacksquare$

**Example 3 (Order of elements in $D_4$).** Find the order of every element.

*Solution.*
- $|1| = 1$
- $|r| = 4$ (since $r^4 = 1$, $r^2 \neq 1$)
- $|r^2| = 2$ ($R_{180°}$: half-turn)
- $|r^3| = 4$
- $|s| = |sr| = |sr^2| = |sr^3| = 2$ (all reflections are involutions)

Five elements of order 2, two elements of order 4, one identity. Total 8. ✓ $\blacksquare$

**Example 4 (Symmetries of a non-regular figure).** What are the symmetries of a **rectangle** that is not a square?

*Solution.* A non-square rectangle has the same symmetries as the square except the $90°$ and $270°$ rotations (which would stretch it). Four symmetries:
- identity
- $R_{180°}$ (half-turn)
- $h$ (horizontal reflection)
- $v$ (vertical reflection)

This group is called the **Klein four-group** $V_4 = \{1, a, b, ab\}$ with $a^2 = b^2 = (ab)^2 = 1$ and $ab = ba$. Note: $V_4$ is **abelian**, unlike $D_n$ for $n \geq 3$. $\blacksquare$

**Example 5 (Chirality / Handedness).** The letter **F** has only the trivial symmetry (identity); the letter **H** has $V_4$-symmetry; the letter **X** has $D_2 = V_4$-symmetry (actually D_4 if we draw it symmetric... depends on font). A stickfigure starfish ($5$-pointed) has $D_5$-symmetry.

## 2.8 Practice Problems

1. Describe the symmetry group of a regular hexagon. How many elements? What are the generators?
2. What is the symmetry group of the letter "Z"?
3. In $D_3$, compute $s_B \circ s_C$.
4. Show that in $D_n$, every element is either a rotation $r^k$ or a reflection $sr^k$ (exactly $n$ of each).
5. Find a figure whose symmetry group is $\mathbb{Z}_3$ (cyclic of order 3). *Hint: remove symmetry by orientation.*
6. Prove that $D_n$ is non-abelian for $n \geq 3$.
7. List all subgroups of $D_4$.

### Solutions

**Solution 1.** $D_6$: 6 rotations ($0°, 60°, 120°, 180°, 240°, 300°$) and 6 reflections (through each pair of opposite vertices, and through each pair of opposite edge midpoints). $|D_6| = 12$. Generators: $r = R_{60°}$ and $s$ = any reflection. Relations: $r^6 = 1$, $s^2 = 1$, $srs = r^{-1}$. $\blacksquare$

**Solution 2.** "Z" has $R_{180°}$ as a symmetry (and identity). So the group is $\mathbb{Z}_2 = C_2 = \{1, R_{180°}\}$. $\blacksquare$

**Solution 3.** $s_B s_C$: $(s_B s_C)(A) = s_B(s_C(A)) = s_B(B) = B$. Wait — $s_C$ swaps $A \leftrightarrow B$ and fixes $C$. So $s_C(A) = B$. Then $s_B$ swaps $A \leftrightarrow C$ and fixes $B$. So $s_B(B) = B$. Thus $(s_B s_C)(A) = B$. $(s_B s_C)(B) = s_B(A) = C$. $(s_B s_C)(C) = s_B(C) = A$. Result: $A \to B \to C \to A$, which is $r_1$. $\blacksquare$

**Solution 4.** Any element $g \in D_n$ is a composition of $r$'s and $s$'s. Using $sr = r^{-1}s$ repeatedly, we can move all $s$'s to the right: any word $s^{\varepsilon_1} r^{k_1} s^{\varepsilon_2} r^{k_2} \cdots$ reduces to $s^\varepsilon r^k$ for some $\varepsilon \in \{0, 1\}$, $k \in \{0, 1, \ldots, n-1\}$. Gives $2n$ distinct elements — equal to $|D_n|$, so no collisions. $\blacksquare$

**Solution 5.** The "triskelion" (three curved legs meeting at a center, all bent in the same direction) has rotational symmetry of order 3 but no reflection (a reflection would reverse the bending). Group: $C_3 = \mathbb{Z}_3$. The flag of the Isle of Man is a classic example. $\blacksquare$

**Solution 6.** In $D_n$ ($n \geq 3$), $sr$ vs $rs$: from $srs = r^{-1}$, we get $sr = r^{-1}s$. If $sr = rs$, then $r^{-1}s = rs$, so $r^{-1} = r$, i.e., $r^2 = 1$. But $|r| = n \geq 3$, contradiction. Hence $sr \neq rs$ and $D_n$ is non-abelian. $\blacksquare$

**Solution 7.** Subgroups of $D_4 = \{1, r, r^2, r^3, s, sr, sr^2, sr^3\}$ (order 8, so subgroups have orders $1, 2, 4, 8$ by Lagrange):
- Order 1: $\{1\}$.
- Order 2: $\{1, r^2\}, \{1, s\}, \{1, sr\}, \{1, sr^2\}, \{1, sr^3\}$ (five).
- Order 4: $\{1, r, r^2, r^3\} \cong \mathbb{Z}_4$; $\{1, r^2, s, sr^2\} \cong V_4$; $\{1, r^2, sr, sr^3\} \cong V_4$.
- Order 8: $D_4$ itself.

Total: 1 + 5 + 3 + 1 = 10 subgroups. The subgroup lattice is discussed in [[12-subgroup-lattice-and-dihedral]]. $\blacksquare$
