# 22. Ideals and Quotient Rings

**Ideals** are the ring-theoretic analog of normal subgroups: subsets of a ring that are invariant under multiplication by any ring element, and which allow the formation of a **quotient ring** $R/I$. This chapter defines ideals, constructs quotient rings, classifies ideals in $\mathbb{Z}$, and introduces the critical concepts of **prime ideal** (quotient is an integral domain) and **maximal ideal** (quotient is a field).

## 22.1 Ideals

> **Definition 22.1 (Ideal).** A subset $I$ of a ring $R$ is an **ideal** (two-sided) if:
> 1. $I$ is a subgroup of $(R, +)$ (i.e., $0 \in I$; $a - b \in I$ whenever $a, b \in I$).
> 2. **Absorption:** For every $r \in R$ and $a \in I$, both $ra \in I$ and $ar \in I$.

For commutative rings, (2) reduces to $ra \in I$.

> **Definition 22.2.** An ideal $I \subseteq R$ is **proper** if $I \neq R$.

> **Proposition 22.3.** An ideal $I$ is proper iff $I$ contains no units.

*Proof.* If $u \in I$ is a unit, then $1 = u^{-1} u \in I$ (by absorption), so for any $r \in R$, $r = r \cdot 1 \in I$, giving $I = R$.

Conversely, if $I = R$, then $1 \in I$ is a unit. $\blacksquare$

### Examples of ideals

**Example 1.** $n\mathbb{Z}$ is an ideal of $\mathbb{Z}$ for any $n \ge 0$.

**Example 2.** $\{0\}$ and $R$ are always ideals (**trivial ideals**).

**Example 3.** In $\mathbb{Z}[x]$, the set of polynomials with even constant term is an ideal: $I = \{p(x) : p(0) \equiv 0 \pmod 2\}$. Equivalently, $I = \langle 2, x \rangle$ (see §22.3).

**Example 4.** In $M_n(R)$, the set of matrices with bottom row zero is **not** an ideal — it's closed under left multiplication but not right.

**Example 5.** In a field $F$, the only ideals are $\{0\}$ and $F$.

*Proof.* If $I \neq \{0\}$, pick $0 \neq a \in I$. Then $a$ is a unit, so $I = F$. $\blacksquare$

## 22.2 Principal Ideals

> **Definition 22.4 (Principal ideal).** In a commutative ring with unity $R$, the **principal ideal generated by $a$** is
> $$\langle a \rangle = Ra = \{ra : r \in R\}.$$

> **Proposition 22.5.** $\langle a \rangle$ is the smallest ideal containing $a$.

### Examples

**Example 6.** In $\mathbb{Z}$, $\langle n \rangle = n\mathbb{Z}$.

**Example 7.** In $\mathbb{Q}$, $\langle 2 \rangle = \mathbb{Q}$ (since $2$ is a unit).

**Example 8.** $\langle 0 \rangle = \{0\}$ (zero ideal). $\langle 1 \rangle = R$ (entire ring).

> **Definition 22.6 (PID).** A **principal ideal domain** (PID) is an integral domain in which every ideal is principal.

**Example 9.** $\mathbb{Z}$ is a PID (Theorem 22.7 below).

**Example 10.** $\mathbb{F}[x]$ for $\mathbb{F}$ a field is a PID (Theorem 24.7 of [[24-polynomial-rings]]).

**Example 11.** $\mathbb{Z}[x]$ is **not** a PID: the ideal $\langle 2, x \rangle$ is not principal.

### Every ideal in $\mathbb{Z}$ is principal

> **Theorem 22.7.** Every ideal of $\mathbb{Z}$ is principal.

*Proof.* Let $I \subseteq \mathbb{Z}$ be an ideal. If $I = \{0\}$, $I = \langle 0 \rangle$.

Otherwise, let $n$ be the smallest positive integer in $I$ (exists by well-ordering). Claim $I = n\mathbb{Z}$.

$n\mathbb{Z} \subseteq I$: by absorption, $kn \in I$ for all $k \in \mathbb{Z}$.

$I \subseteq n\mathbb{Z}$: for any $m \in I$, divide by $n$: $m = nq + r$ with $0 \le r < n$. Then $r = m - nq \in I$. By minimality of $n$ and $0 \le r < n$, $r = 0$, so $m = nq \in n\mathbb{Z}$. $\blacksquare$

## 22.3 Ideals Generated by Subsets

> **Definition 22.8.** For a subset $S \subseteq R$ of a commutative ring with unity, the ideal generated by $S$ is
> $$\langle S \rangle = \left\{ \sum_{i=1}^n r_i s_i : r_i \in R,\ s_i \in S,\ n \in \mathbb{N} \right\}.$$

**Example 12.** In $\mathbb{Z}$: $\langle 6, 10 \rangle = \gcd(6, 10)\mathbb{Z} = 2\mathbb{Z}$ (since $2 = -1 \cdot 6 + 1 \cdot 8 = -2 \cdot 6 + 2 \cdot 10$... wait, $-2 \cdot 6 + 2 \cdot 10 = -12 + 20 = 8$. Try $6 - 4 = 2$, but we need integer combos: $(-3)(6) + 2(10) = -18 + 20 = 2$ ✓). So $\langle 6, 10 \rangle = \langle 2 \rangle$.

More generally, $\langle m, n \rangle = \langle \gcd(m, n) \rangle$ in $\mathbb{Z}$.

**Example 13.** In $\mathbb{Z}[x]$: $\langle 2, x \rangle$ contains $2, x$, hence $2r(x) + xs(x)$ for all $r, s \in \mathbb{Z}[x]$. These are polynomials with even constant term.

## 22.4 Quotient Rings

> **Theorem 22.9 (Quotient ring construction).** Let $I \trianglelefteq R$ be an ideal. The set of cosets $R/I = \{a + I : a \in R\}$ forms a ring under:
> $$(a + I) + (b + I) = (a + b) + I$$
> $$(a + I)(b + I) = ab + I.$$

*Proof of well-definedness of multiplication.* If $a + I = a' + I$ and $b + I = b' + I$, then $a - a' \in I$ and $b - b' \in I$. Compute:
$$ab - a'b' = ab - a'b + a'b - a'b' = (a - a')b + a'(b - b').$$
Since $I$ is an ideal, $(a - a')b \in I$ and $a'(b - b') \in I$. So $ab - a'b' \in I$, i.e., $ab + I = a'b' + I$. $\blacksquare$

The remaining ring axioms follow directly from those in $R$.

> **Proposition 22.10.** The map $\pi: R \to R/I$, $\pi(a) = a + I$, is a surjective ring homomorphism with $\ker \pi = I$. (This is the **canonical projection**.)

### Examples

**Example 14.** $\mathbb{Z}/n\mathbb{Z} = \mathbb{Z}_n$. The ring of integers modulo $n$ is the quotient of $\mathbb{Z}$ by the ideal $n\mathbb{Z}$.

**Example 15.** $\mathbb{R}[x]/\langle x^2 + 1 \rangle \cong \mathbb{C}$.

*Proof.* $\mathbb{R}[x] \to \mathbb{C}$ by $p(x) \mapsto p(i)$. Kernel: $p(i) = 0$ iff $(x - i) \mid p(x)$ in $\mathbb{C}[x]$ iff $(x^2 + 1) \mid p(x)$ in $\mathbb{R}[x]$ (using that $(x - i)(x + i) = x^2 + 1$ and real polynomials have conjugate roots together).

Surjective: any $a + bi = a \cdot 1 + b \cdot i = \pi(a + bx)$ ... wait, we want $a + bi$, so $p(x) = a + bx$ gives $p(i) = a + bi$. ✓

First Isomorphism Theorem (ring version): $\mathbb{R}[x]/\langle x^2 + 1 \rangle \cong \mathbb{C}$. $\blacksquare$

**Example 16.** $\mathbb{Z}[x]/\langle x \rangle \cong \mathbb{Z}$ (evaluation at 0).

**Example 17.** $\mathbb{Z}[i]/\langle 1 + i \rangle \cong \mathbb{F}_2$ (Example 16 of [[21-integral-domains]]).

## 22.5 The Correspondence Theorem for Rings

> **Theorem 22.11 (Correspondence).** Let $I \trianglelefteq R$. Ideals of $R/I$ correspond bijectively to ideals of $R$ containing $I$, via $\bar{J} \leftrightarrow \pi^{-1}(\bar{J}) = J$.

(Same principle as for groups; see [[18-isomorphism-theorems]].)

**Example 18.** Ideals of $\mathbb{Z}/12\mathbb{Z}$ correspond to ideals of $\mathbb{Z}$ containing $12\mathbb{Z}$. These are $d\mathbb{Z}$ for $d \mid 12$: $\mathbb{Z}, 2\mathbb{Z}, 3\mathbb{Z}, 4\mathbb{Z}, 6\mathbb{Z}, 12\mathbb{Z}$. So $\mathbb{Z}_{12}$ has 6 ideals.

## 22.6 Prime and Maximal Ideals

> **Definition 22.12 (Prime ideal).** In a commutative ring $R$, an ideal $P \subsetneq R$ is **prime** if
> $$ab \in P \implies a \in P \text{ or } b \in P.$$

> **Definition 22.13 (Maximal ideal).** An ideal $M \subsetneq R$ is **maximal** if there is no ideal $J$ with $M \subsetneq J \subsetneq R$.

### Characterization via quotient rings

> **Theorem 22.14.** Let $R$ be a commutative ring with unity, $I \subsetneq R$. Then:
> 1. $I$ is prime $\iff R/I$ is an **integral domain**.
> 2. $I$ is maximal $\iff R/I$ is a **field**.

*Proof.*

**(1).** $R/I$ is an integral domain iff $R/I$ has no zero divisors iff $(a + I)(b + I) = I \Rightarrow a + I = I$ or $b + I = I$ iff $ab \in I \Rightarrow a \in I$ or $b \in I$ iff $I$ is prime.

**(2).** $R/I$ is a field iff every nonzero element of $R/I$ is a unit iff for every $a \notin I$, there exists $b \in R$ with $ab - 1 \in I$ iff the ideal $I + \langle a \rangle = R$ for every $a \notin I$ iff $I$ is maximal. $\blacksquare$

### Prime vs maximal

> **Corollary 22.15.** In a commutative ring with unity, every maximal ideal is prime.

*Proof.* Field ⟹ integral domain. $\blacksquare$

**Converse fails.** Example: $\langle 0 \rangle \subsetneq \mathbb{Z}$ is prime (since $\mathbb{Z}/\{0\} = \mathbb{Z}$ is an integral domain) but not maximal (contained in e.g. $\langle 2 \rangle$).

### In $\mathbb{Z}$

> **Theorem 22.16.** Prime ideals of $\mathbb{Z}$: $\{0\}$ and $\langle p \rangle$ for primes $p$. Maximal ideals: $\langle p \rangle$ for primes $p$.

*Proof.* $\mathbb{Z}/\langle n \rangle = \mathbb{Z}_n$ is:
- Integral domain iff $n = 0$ or $n$ prime.
- Field iff $n$ prime.

So prime ideals are $\{0\}$ and $\langle p \rangle$ for $p$ prime. Maximal ideals are only $\langle p \rangle$ for $p$ prime ($\{0\}$ is not maximal). $\blacksquare$

## 22.7 Operations on Ideals

Given ideals $I, J \subseteq R$:

- **Sum:** $I + J = \{a + b : a \in I, b \in J\}$ — the smallest ideal containing both.
- **Intersection:** $I \cap J$ — ideal.
- **Product:** $IJ = \{\sum_{\text{finite}} a_i b_i : a_i \in I, b_i \in J\}$ — ideal.

**Example 19.** In $\mathbb{Z}$:
- $\langle 6 \rangle + \langle 10 \rangle = \langle \gcd(6, 10) \rangle = \langle 2 \rangle$
- $\langle 6 \rangle \cap \langle 10 \rangle = \langle \operatorname{lcm}(6, 10) \rangle = \langle 30 \rangle$
- $\langle 6 \rangle \cdot \langle 10 \rangle = \langle 60 \rangle$

## 22.8 Nilradical and Jacobson Radical (Preview)

Without full treatment (these are more advanced):

- **Nilradical** $\operatorname{Nil}(R)$ = intersection of all prime ideals = set of nilpotent elements.
- **Jacobson radical** $J(R)$ = intersection of all maximal ideals.

These are key tools in commutative algebra.

## 22.9 Practice Problems

**Problem 1.** Show that the set $I = \{2a + 4bi : a, b \in \mathbb{Z}\}$ is **not** an ideal of $\mathbb{Z}[i]$.

**Problem 2.** List all ideals of $\mathbb{Z}_{24}$.

**Problem 3.** Is $\langle 2, x \rangle$ a prime ideal of $\mathbb{Z}[x]$? A maximal ideal?

**Problem 4.** Show that $\langle x \rangle$ is a prime ideal of $\mathbb{Z}[x]$ but not maximal.

**Problem 5.** Compute $\mathbb{R}[x]/\langle x^2 - 1 \rangle$.

**Problem 6.** Show that in a finite commutative ring, every prime ideal is maximal.

**Problem 7.** Determine whether $\mathbb{Z}[x]/\langle x^2 + 1 \rangle$ is an integral domain.

### Solutions

**1.** $I = \{2a + 4bi\}$: closed under $+$? $(2a_1 + 4b_1 i) + (2a_2 + 4b_2 i) = 2(a_1 + a_2) + 4(b_1 + b_2)i$ ✓.

Absorption: take $r = i \in \mathbb{Z}[i]$, $a = 2 \in I$ (with $b = 0$). Then $ra = 2i$. Is $2i \in I$? Need $2a + 4bi = 2i$, i.e., $a = 0, b = 1/2 \notin \mathbb{Z}$. So $2i \notin I$. **Not an ideal.** $\blacksquare$

**2.** Ideals of $\mathbb{Z}_{24}$ ↔ ideals of $\mathbb{Z}$ containing $24\mathbb{Z}$ ↔ $d\mathbb{Z}$ for $d \mid 24$. Divisors of 24: $\{1, 2, 3, 4, 6, 8, 12, 24\}$. 8 ideals. $\boxed{8}$

**3.** $\mathbb{Z}[x]/\langle 2, x \rangle$: modding out 2 gives $\mathbb{F}_2[x]$, then modding out $x$ gives $\mathbb{F}_2$. So quotient is $\mathbb{F}_2$, a field. Hence **$\langle 2, x \rangle$ is maximal** (and therefore prime). $\boxed{}$

**4.** $\mathbb{Z}[x]/\langle x \rangle \cong \mathbb{Z}$ (evaluation at 0). $\mathbb{Z}$ is an integral domain but not a field. So $\langle x \rangle$ is prime but not maximal. $\blacksquare$

**5.** $x^2 - 1 = (x - 1)(x + 1)$ in $\mathbb{R}[x]$. By CRT for rings: $\mathbb{R}[x]/\langle x^2 - 1 \rangle \cong \mathbb{R}[x]/\langle x - 1 \rangle \times \mathbb{R}[x]/\langle x + 1 \rangle \cong \mathbb{R} \times \mathbb{R}$. $\boxed{\mathbb{R} \times \mathbb{R}}$

**6.** Let $R$ be finite commutative ring, $P$ prime. Then $R/P$ is a finite integral domain (Theorem 21.4), hence a field. So $P$ is maximal. $\blacksquare$

**7.** $\mathbb{Z}[x]/\langle x^2 + 1 \rangle \cong \mathbb{Z}[i]$ via $x \mapsto i$. Gaussian integers are an integral domain. **Yes, integral domain.** $\boxed{\cong \mathbb{Z}[i]}$
