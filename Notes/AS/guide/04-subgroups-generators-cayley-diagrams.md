# 4. Subgroups, Generators, and Cayley Diagrams

> **Where we are.** We have defined groups. To understand a group, the single most important tool is to look at its **subgroups** — subsets that are themselves groups. A subgroup "inside" $G$ is like a subspace inside a vector space: it inherits structure while being simpler. The full list of subgroups of $G$ is called the **subgroup lattice**, and it often fully determines the group.

---

## 4.1 Subgroups

> **Definition (Subgroup).** A **subgroup** of a group $G$ is a non-empty subset $H \subseteq G$ that is itself a group under the operation inherited from $G$. We write $H \leq G$.

Always: $\{e\} \leq G$ (trivial subgroup) and $G \leq G$ (improper subgroup). All others are **proper nontrivial** subgroups.

### The subgroup test

> **Theorem 4.1 (Two-step subgroup test).** A non-empty subset $H \subseteq G$ is a subgroup if and only if:
> 1. (Closure) $a, b \in H \Rightarrow ab \in H$.
> 2. (Inverses) $a \in H \Rightarrow a^{-1} \in H$.
>
> *Proof ($\Leftarrow$).* Associativity is inherited from $G$. Take any $a \in H$. Then $a^{-1} \in H$ (axiom 2), so $e = a a^{-1} \in H$ (axiom 1). Identity ✓, inverses ✓, closure ✓, associativity ✓. So $H$ is a group.
>
> $(\Rightarrow)$ Immediate. $\blacksquare$

> **Theorem 4.2 (One-step subgroup test).** A non-empty $H \subseteq G$ is a subgroup if and only if
> $$\forall a, b \in H : ab^{-1} \in H.$$
>
> *Proof.* If $H$ is a subgroup, then $b^{-1} \in H$ and $ab^{-1} \in H$. Conversely, non-empty means $\exists a \in H$; take $b = a$ to get $e = aa^{-1} \in H$. Taking $a = e$: $b \in H \Rightarrow e b^{-1} = b^{-1} \in H$. Then $a, b \in H \Rightarrow a, b^{-1} \in H \Rightarrow a(b^{-1})^{-1} = ab \in H$. $\blacksquare$

> **Theorem 4.3 (Finite subgroup test).** If $G$ is a finite group and $H \subseteq G$ is non-empty and closed under the operation, then $H \leq G$.
>
> *Proof.* Given closure, we only need inverses. For $a \in H$, look at $a, a^2, a^3, \ldots$; all in $H$ by closure. $H$ finite, so $a^i = a^j$ for some $i < j$; then $a^{j-i} = e \in H$, and $a^{-1} = a^{j-i-1} \in H$ (note $j - i - 1 \geq 0$, and if $j - i - 1 = 0$, $a = e$). $\blacksquare$

## 4.2 Examples of Subgroups

**Example 1.** $(\mathbb{Z}, +) \leq (\mathbb{Q}, +) \leq (\mathbb{R}, +) \leq (\mathbb{C}, +)$.

**Example 2.** $n\mathbb{Z} = \{nk : k \in \mathbb{Z}\} \leq \mathbb{Z}$ for each $n \geq 0$. These are **all** subgroups of $(\mathbb{Z}, +)$.

**Example 3.** In $D_4$: subgroups include $\{1, r^2\}$, $\{1, r, r^2, r^3\}$ (rotations), $\{1, s\}$, and more (full list in [[12-subgroup-lattice-and-dihedral]]).

**Example 4.** $SL_n(\mathbb{R}) \leq GL_n(\mathbb{R})$ (kernel of determinant).

**Example 5.** $A_n \leq S_n$ (even permutations; kernel of sign). See [[05-permutation-and-dihedral-groups]].

**Example 6.** Center: $Z(G) = \{a \in G : ag = ga \; \forall g \in G\}$ is a subgroup of $G$. (Verify: if $a, b \in Z(G)$, then $ab \cdot g = a g b = g a b$, so $ab \in Z(G)$; $a^{-1}g = a^{-1}(ga)a^{-1} = a^{-1}ag a^{-1} = ga^{-1}$, so $a^{-1} \in Z(G)$.) Covered in [[16-centralizer-normalizer-stabilizer]].

**Example 7 (Non-example).** $\{\text{odd integers}\}$ is **not** a subgroup of $(\mathbb{Z}, +)$: it doesn't contain $0$, and is not closed ($1 + 1 = 2$ even).

## 4.3 Generated Subgroups

> **Definition.** Let $S \subseteq G$. The **subgroup generated by $S$**, written $\langle S \rangle$, is the smallest subgroup of $G$ containing $S$.

Equivalently: the set of all finite products $s_1^{\varepsilon_1} s_2^{\varepsilon_2} \cdots s_k^{\varepsilon_k}$ with $s_i \in S$ and $\varepsilon_i \in \{\pm 1\}$ (plus the identity $e$, which is the empty product).

**Special cases:**
- $\langle \emptyset \rangle = \{e\}$
- $\langle a \rangle = \{a^n : n \in \mathbb{Z}\}$ — the **cyclic subgroup** generated by $a$. See [[06-cyclic-groups-and-order]].
- $\langle a, b \rangle$ = set of all words in $a, b, a^{-1}, b^{-1}$.

> **Theorem 4.4.** The intersection of any collection of subgroups is a subgroup.
>
> *Proof.* Let $\{H_i\}_{i \in I}$ be subgroups of $G$. $H = \bigcap H_i$. $e \in$ each $H_i$, so $e \in H$, non-empty. If $a, b \in H$, then $a, b \in H_i$ for each $i$, so $ab^{-1} \in H_i$ for each $i$, hence $ab^{-1} \in H$. By one-step test, $H \leq G$. $\blacksquare$

> **Corollary 4.5.** $\langle S \rangle = \bigcap \{H \leq G : S \subseteq H\}$ (intersection of all subgroups containing $S$).

**Union of subgroups.** In contrast, the *union* of two subgroups is **rarely** a subgroup: $H \cup K \leq G$ iff $H \subseteq K$ or $K \subseteq H$.

## 4.4 Generators and Defining Relations — Presentations

A group can be described by giving **generators** and **relations**. The notation:
$$G = \langle g_1, g_2, \ldots, g_n \mid r_1 = 1, r_2 = 1, \ldots \rangle$$
means: $G$ is generated by $g_1, \ldots, g_n$ subject only to the stated relations (and their consequences).

**Example 8 (Cyclic group).** $\mathbb{Z}_n = \langle a \mid a^n = 1 \rangle$.

**Example 9 (Dihedral group).** $D_n = \langle r, s \mid r^n = 1, s^2 = 1, srs = r^{-1} \rangle$.

**Example 10 (Klein four).** $V_4 = \langle a, b \mid a^2 = b^2 = (ab)^2 = 1 \rangle$.

**Example 11 (Quaternion).** $Q_8 = \langle i, j \mid i^4 = 1, i^2 = j^2, jij^{-1} = i^{-1} \rangle$. (8 elements: $\pm 1, \pm i, \pm j, \pm ij$.)

**Example 12 (Symmetric group $S_3$).** $S_3 = \langle a, b \mid a^3 = b^2 = 1, bab = a^{-1} \rangle$ — same presentation as $D_3$! So $S_3 \cong D_3$.

**Warning.** Deciding whether two presentations give isomorphic groups is algorithmically undecidable in general (**word problem**). For small finite groups we can just enumerate elements.

## 4.5 Cayley Diagrams

> **Definition.** The **Cayley diagram** of $G$ with respect to generators $S$ is a directed colored graph with:
> - **Vertices**: elements of $G$.
> - **Edges**: for each $s \in S$, a colored directed edge from $g$ to $gs$ for every $g \in G$.

Cayley diagrams visualize the group: multiplying by a generator is "following an arrow of the corresponding color."

### Cayley diagram of $\mathbb{Z}_4$

Generator $a$ of order 4. Diagram is a 4-cycle:
$$
e \xrightarrow{a} a \xrightarrow{a} a^2 \xrightarrow{a} a^3 \xrightarrow{a} e
$$

### Cayley diagram of $V_4$

Generators $a, b$ (each of order 2). Diagram is a 4-cycle with "rungs" — a square where opposite vertices are also connected:
```
    e ——a—— a
    |       |
    b       b
    |       |
    b ——a—— ab
```
(Since $a, b$ are involutions, both arrows are bidirectional.)

### Cayley diagram of $D_3$

Generators $r$ (rotation, order 3) and $s$ (reflection, order 2). Two interleaved triangles:

- Outer triangle: $e \to r \to r^2 \to e$ via $r$-edges.
- Inner triangle: $s \to sr \to sr^2 \to s$ via $r$-edges.
- Connections: $s$-edge joins $e \leftrightarrow s$, $r \leftrightarrow sr$, $r^2 \leftrightarrow sr^2$.

A prism-like figure with 6 vertices.

### Cayley diagram of $Q_8$

4D "hypercube-like" structure; traditionally drawn as two interleaved squares with extra edges — harder to visualize.

## 4.6 Worked Examples

**Example 13 (Subgroups of $\mathbb{Z}$).** Prove that every subgroup of $(\mathbb{Z}, +)$ is of the form $n\mathbb{Z}$ for some $n \geq 0$.

*Solution.* Let $H \leq \mathbb{Z}$. If $H = \{0\}$, take $n = 0$. Else $H$ contains a positive integer (if $a \in H$, $a \neq 0$, then $-a \in H$, so positive elements exist). Let $n = \min \{h > 0 : h \in H\}$. Claim: $H = n\mathbb{Z}$.

$(\supseteq)$: $n \in H$, so $\pm n, \pm 2n, \ldots \in H$. $(\subseteq)$: take $h \in H$. Division algorithm: $h = qn + r$ with $0 \leq r < n$. Then $r = h - qn \in H$ (closure), and $0 \leq r < n$. By minimality of $n$, $r = 0$. So $h = qn \in n\mathbb{Z}$. $\blacksquare$

**Remark.** This result is the group-theoretic basis of the Euclidean algorithm: $\gcd(a, b)$ is the positive generator of $\langle a, b \rangle \subseteq \mathbb{Z}$, hence $\gcd(a, b)$ is a $\mathbb{Z}$-linear combination of $a, b$ (Bezout).

**Example 14 (Subgroups of $\mathbb{Z}_{12}$).** Find all subgroups.

*Solution.* Subgroups of cyclic $\mathbb{Z}_n$ correspond to divisors of $n$ (proved in [[06-cyclic-groups-and-order]]). Divisors of 12: 1, 2, 3, 4, 6, 12. Subgroups:
- $\langle 0 \rangle = \{0\}$ (order 1)
- $\langle 6 \rangle = \{0, 6\}$ (order 2)
- $\langle 4 \rangle = \{0, 4, 8\}$ (order 3)
- $\langle 3 \rangle = \{0, 3, 6, 9\}$ (order 4)
- $\langle 2 \rangle = \{0, 2, 4, 6, 8, 10\}$ (order 6)
- $\langle 1 \rangle = \mathbb{Z}_{12}$ (order 12)

Six subgroups. $\blacksquare$

**Example 15 (Intersection).** In $\mathbb{Z}$, compute $4\mathbb{Z} \cap 6\mathbb{Z}$.

*Solution.* $n \in 4\mathbb{Z} \cap 6\mathbb{Z}$ iff $4 \mid n$ and $6 \mid n$, iff $\text{lcm}(4, 6) = 12 \mid n$. So $4\mathbb{Z} \cap 6\mathbb{Z} = 12 \mathbb{Z}$. $\blacksquare$

**Example 16 (Generation).** In $\mathbb{Z}$, compute $\langle 4, 6 \rangle$.

*Solution.* $\langle 4, 6 \rangle$ = all $\mathbb{Z}$-linear combinations of 4 and 6 = all multiples of $\gcd(4, 6) = 2$ = $2\mathbb{Z}$. $\blacksquare$

**Example 17 (Subgroup of $S_3$).** List subgroups of $S_3$.

*Solution.* $|S_3| = 6$. Possible subgroup orders: 1, 2, 3, 6.
- Order 1: $\{e\}$
- Order 2: $\{e, (1\,2)\}, \{e, (1\,3)\}, \{e, (2\,3)\}$ (three)
- Order 3: $A_3 = \{e, (1\,2\,3), (1\,3\,2)\}$ (one)
- Order 6: $S_3$

Total 6 subgroups. $\blacksquare$

**Example 18 (Using 4.2).** Show $H = \{a + b\sqrt{2} : a, b \in \mathbb{Z}\}$ is a subgroup of $(\mathbb{R}, +)$.

*Solution.* $0 \in H$ (take $a = b = 0$), non-empty. For $x = a + b\sqrt 2$, $y = c + d\sqrt 2 \in H$:
$$x - y = (a - c) + (b - d)\sqrt 2 \in H.$$
By one-step test, $H \leq \mathbb{R}$. $\blacksquare$

## 4.7 Practice Problems

1. Show that $H = \{n \in \mathbb{Z} : n \text{ even}\}$ is a subgroup of $\mathbb{Z}$.
2. Is $H = \{A \in GL_2(\mathbb{R}) : \det A = \pm 1\}$ a subgroup?
3. Find all subgroups of $V_4$.
4. List all subgroups of $\mathbb{Z}_{20}$.
5. Prove that $H \cup K$ is a subgroup iff $H \subseteq K$ or $K \subseteq H$.
6. In $S_4$, find the subgroup generated by $(1\,2)$ and $(1\,2\,3\,4)$. How many elements?
7. Prove: if $G$ has only finitely many subgroups, then $G$ is finite.

### Solutions

**Solution 1.** $H = 2\mathbb{Z}$. $0 \in H$. If $2m, 2n \in H$, then $2m - 2n = 2(m-n) \in H$. Subgroup. $\blacksquare$

**Solution 2.** Non-empty ($I \in H$). For $A, B \in H$: $\det(AB^{-1}) = \det A / \det B = \pm 1 / \pm 1 = \pm 1$. So $AB^{-1} \in H$. Subgroup. $\blacksquare$

**Solution 3.** $V_4 = \{e, a, b, ab\}$: subgroups
- $\{e\}$
- $\{e, a\}, \{e, b\}, \{e, ab\}$
- $V_4$ itself.
Five subgroups. $\blacksquare$

**Solution 4.** Divisors of 20: 1, 2, 4, 5, 10, 20. Subgroups: $\langle 0 \rangle, \langle 10 \rangle, \langle 5 \rangle, \langle 4 \rangle, \langle 2 \rangle, \mathbb{Z}_{20}$. Six subgroups. $\blacksquare$

**Solution 5.** ($\Leftarrow$): if $H \subseteq K$, $H \cup K = K$, a subgroup. ($\Rightarrow$): suppose $H \not\subseteq K$ and $K \not\subseteq H$. Pick $h \in H \setminus K$, $k \in K \setminus H$. Then $hk \in H \cup K$. If $hk \in H$: $k = h^{-1}(hk) \in H$, contradiction. If $hk \in K$: $h = (hk)k^{-1} \in K$, contradiction. So $H \cup K$ is not closed. $\blacksquare$

**Solution 6.** $\langle (1\,2), (1\,2\,3\,4) \rangle$: since $(1\,2\,3\,4)$ generates the cyclic rotation of order 4, and adding a reflection-like transposition $(1\,2)$... compute: $(1\,2)(1\,2\,3\,4) = (2\,3\,4)$... actually in $S_4$, $\langle (1\,2), (1\,2\,3\,4) \rangle = S_4$ itself (size 24). Alternatively, this is the standard way to generate $S_n$ from an adjacent transposition and an $n$-cycle. $\blacksquare$

**Solution 7.** Contrapositive: suppose $G$ infinite. If $G$ has an element $a$ of infinite order, then $\langle a \rangle, \langle a^2 \rangle, \langle a^4 \rangle, \ldots$ are infinitely many distinct subgroups (each $\langle a^{2^k} \rangle$ is strictly larger as $k$ decreases). If every element has finite order but $G$ is infinite, pick $a_1, a_2, \ldots$ with $a_{i+1} \notin \langle a_1, \ldots, a_i \rangle$; each inclusion strict, so infinitely many distinct subgroups. $\blacksquare$
